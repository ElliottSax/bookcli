Chapter 11: Setting the Guillotine

The air in the attic was a solid thing. It was dust and old wood, the ghost of turpentine, and the sweet, decaying scent of dried lavender sachets stuffed into forgotten corners by Simone long ago. Léo breathed it in, each inhalation a gritty reminder that this pocket of stillness was stolen, temporary. The only light came from a single, dust-caked dormer window, through which the Parisian afternoon bled a tired, grey-gold pallor. It illuminated motes dancing like static, and the sharp, fearful planes of his daughter’s face.
Claire sat on a moth-eaten chaise longue, her knees drawn to her chest. She was too old for the posture, too young for the shadows under her eyes. The worn velvet creaked softly beneath her as she shifted, releasing a faint perfume of old fabric and cedar. She watched him, this stranger who was her father, as he knelt on the bare floorboards, the ledger splayed open before him like a dissected heart.
“It’s not just names and numbers, Claire,” he said, his voice a low rasp that was swallowed by the woolly silence. He traced a column with a grimy finger. “This... this entry here. 'Project Cerberus. Site Gamma. Disposal via Channel 7.' Channel 7 was a meat-packing plant in Clichy. They found three workers in one of the industrial mincers. The official report said a tragic accident, a safety failure. It wasn’t. It was a clean-up. Saint-Clair’s people needed the site sterile.”

Claire flinched. Her world had been lectures, student protests, the theoretical corruption of distant men. Not this. Never this.
The aito thicken with images she'd never associated with her father – ruined workers, statisticized death, erasure on an industrial scale. She looked closer, and saw the faint lines around Léo's eyes, the remarkable tiredness in his shoulders. This cruelty danced between worlds, behind pseudo- academic jargon.
“And this,” Léo continued, relentless, turning a page that crackled with menace. “'Roux. Contingency payment. Rue des Rosiers incident.' That was a bookstore run by a journalist who was asking about defense contract overruns. A gas main 'explosion'. The journalist, his wife, their two-year-old daughter. Gone. Paid for by the man who sends you birthday cheques, Claire. Your Uncle Armand.”

“Stop,” she whispered, but the word had no force.
Léo slid his finger across the page, negligence sprinkling atop his grim forbearance. He stopped before a collapsed grant number. A station in limbo.
