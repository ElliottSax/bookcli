Chapter 10: The Oven Spring

The sourdough starter, fed with rye flour and rainwater, bubbled like a peat bog in its glass jar. Elara Finch watched it, her reflection warped in the curved glass. It was a living thing, this starter—a century-old legacy of wild yeast and bacteria passed from Arthur Crofton’s hands, to her grandmother’s, to hers. Now, it was the heart of a trap.

Outside the kitchen window of The Bitter End, Main Street was being dressed for the Harvest Festival. Bunting in gold and crimson flapped against a sky the color of cold slate. Pumpkins lined the sidewalks, their cheerful grins looking, to Elara Finch, like grimaces. The air smelled of woodsmoke from the festival’s newly constructed brick oven, and beneath it, the damp, mineral scent of the coming frost.

“It’s time,” Leo said, entering from the back. He carried a brown paper bag, its contents clinking softly. He looked exhausted, shadows pooled under eyes that wouldn’t quite meet hers. The revelation about his mother, about the hidden alliance between the Crofts and the Huckles, hung between them like a pane of smoked glass. They could see each other through it, but everything was blurred, darkened.

“Did you get everything?” Elara Finch asked, her voice quieter than she intended. Her words fell like dust motes into the thick air between them. She felt it, a magnetic attraction—still doorways left in this crumbling edifice, fault lines waiting to fault.

He nodded, unpacking the bag with a reverence usually reserved for holy relics. “The white flour, milled from the last of the Sweetwater family’s private wheat reserve. Took some ‘donating to the festival’ persuasion.” It was a fine, almost blue-tinged powder. “The honey.” He placed a small, elegant jar on the counter. The label, in florid script, read Huckle Haven Clover & Fireweed. It was the color of old amber, thick and slow. “The salt.” This was in a plain muslin pouch. Grey, flaky, hand-harvested from the flats near the old quarry owned, through a labyrinth of LLCs, by the mayor’s brother-in-law. “And the water.” He set down a canteen. “Drawn this morning from the spring on what used to be Arthur Crofton’s north pasture. Now part of the Gantry land trust.”

Each ingredient was a piece of the map. The flour from the Sweetwaters’ ambition, the honey from the Huckles’ poisoned legacy, the salt from the hidden corruption, the water from the stolen land. And the starter, the living history, the Croft family’s own complicity.

“Your mother?” Elara Finch finally asked, kneading the stiff levain into the autolysed flour and water. Her hands moved with an ease born of years of practice, instinct guiding them. But her words hesitated, caught behind teeth jammed tight with everything.

Leo’s jaw tightened. “She won’t speak to me. Just sits in her sunroom, staring at the mountain. She looks… small.” He picked up the jar of honey, turning it in the light. “All these years, she let me believe Arthur Crofton was a monster. She let the town believe it. To protect what? A deal? A name?” His frustration seeped from his pores like morning dew from a ready-cut lawn.

“To protect you, maybe,” Elara Finch said, though the words felt thin. Her words scratched against his frozen face like peeling away dead skin. She dared to reach out and put a gentle hand on his forearm, letting her touch speak more volumes than her words ever could. “Or the illusion of stability. Some people will do anything to keep the pot from boiling over, even if it means letting the stew spoil from within.”

The dough came together, a shaggy mass in the bowl. Elara Finch began to fold in the salt, then the honey, drizzling its golden sweetness slowly. The conspiracy, made tangible. It was a heavy dough, dense with symbolism and spite. She worked it in silence, the only sound the sticky pull of gluten and the distant thump of a hammer from the festival square.

“He’s here, you know,” Leo said after a while, pulling his arm free, unwilling to lose the ease of the gesture. “The ‘missing’ boy. He’s staying at the motor lodge out on the highway. Calls himself Robert Vale now.”

Elara Finch remembered the man they’d tracked down—a quiet, nervous accountant from Spokane with Arthur Crofton’s same anxious blue eyes. He hadn’t been bribed, he’d been paid off. A monthly stipend, a new identity, a sealed file, all arranged by a lawyer whose name made both Sweetwater and Huckle flinch. His testimony, given in a trembling voice over bad motel coffee, was the final piece. Arthur Crofton hadn’t killed a boy. He’d discovered one—a Huckle son from a concealed affair—and had tried to use the knowledge as use to stop the land grab. It backfired spectacularly.

“He’s agreed to be there today,” Elara Finch said, turning the dough out for its final fold. “When the time is right.”

“If the time is right,” Leo corrected, his gaze on the window. “They know we’re doing this. They’re scared. Scared people are unpredictable.”

Elara Finch shaped the dough into a round, the surface tense and smooth as a drum. She placed it in a linen-lined banneton, the spiral of the cane imprinting a final, target-like pattern on its surface. She covered it with a cloth. “The oven is already lit. The trap is set. All we can do now is spring it.”
The town square was a study in forced merriment. The wood-fired oven, a hulding brick dome, glowed like a furnace heart at the center. Children darted between stalls selling apple butter and knitted scarves, their laughter sharp against the tense backdrop. The entire population of Bitterroot seemed to be there, their attention split between the pie-eating contest and the curious spectacle at the oven.

Elara Finch and Leo stood behind a long trestle table. The shaped loaf, dusted with flour from the Sweetwater reserve, sat between them like an exhibit. A microphone had been set up. Sheriff Lowry lingered at the edge of the crowd, his face a mask of professional neutrality, but his hand never strayed far from his belt.

Elara Finch took a breath, the cold air biting her lungs. She tapped the mic, a single knocking against its wire grid, audible to all. A screech of feedback silenced the square.

“People of Bitterroot,” she began, her voice echoing off the storefronts, their creaking wooden signs like surfaces launched on a fresh plane of imperturbable awareness. “For decades, you’ve celebrated this harvest, this land. And for decades, you’ve lived with a ghost. The ghost of Alistair Arthur Crofton. A murderer, you were told. A monster who stole a child and was killed for it.”

The crowd stood entranced. Not a whisper of breath varied. They wanted a tale that seemed out of place. A crescendo of calm sounds waited out the flutter of fires fueled by seasons nearly spent.
