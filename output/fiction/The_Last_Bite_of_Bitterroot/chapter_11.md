The air in the Bitterroot Bakery was not merely dark; it was a thick, velvety gloom that appeared to swallow the weak afternoon light struggling through the grimy front windows. It was heavy with the scent of old bread—a sour, yeasty ghost of nourishment—and the fine, choking dust of disuse. Elara stood just inside the threshold, the brass bell above the door letting out a final, forlorn *ting* that was absorbed instantly by the silence. Her boots, scuffed from the gravel path, made no sound on the worn oak floorboards as she stepped forward, each footfall a trespass.
The silence was a physical presence. It pressed against her eardrums, a hollow roar that made her own breathing seem obscenely loud. Her eyes, wide and adjusting, moved over the corpse of the place. The long counter, once a gleaming expanse of maple where floury hands had slapped down warm, paper-wrapped bundles, was now a barren plane, dull and filmed with neglect. The shelves behind it, which should have been groaning under the weight of crusty sourdough rounds, braided challahs, and Arthur’s famous rye, were skeletal, holding only shadows and a few forgotten crumbs that looked like mortar in the dim light. The great brick ovens, the twin hearts of the bakery, were cold and dark. Their iron doors were shut, their viewing windows black, empty eyes.
The only sound was the faint, persistent hum of the refrigerator in the back room—a low, steady drone that was less a noise and more a vibration in the teeth, the last mechanical heartbeat of a place in a deep, unnatural sleep. It echoed in the quiet space, a monotonous testament to something still technically alive, but utterly devoid of spirit.
