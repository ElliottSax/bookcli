Elara Finch stood in the doorway, the dusty, flour-dusted workbenches and the gaping maw of the brick oven stretching before her like a skeletal hand. Moonlight, thin and milky, filtered through the grime-caked windows, casting an eerie glow on the scene. Her hunch had been a physical pull, a cord tied behind her navel, dragging her from the chaos of the sabotaged festival tent, past the blinking lights of the sheriff's cruiser, and back here. To where it all began. The air inside the bakery was heavy with the weight of unspoken words, the silence between Elara Finch and Maureen a palpable thing that seemed to vibrate with tension. Elara Finch's eyes locked onto Maureen's, searching for a glimmer of the woman she once knew, the woman who had been a friend, a confidante, and a fellow baker. But Maureen's face was a mask of weary resignation, her eyes sunken and her skin pale. The faint scent of lavender wafted from her hair, a reminder of the perfume she used to wear, a fragrance that had always brought a sense of calm to the bakery. “You found it,” Elara Finch said, her voice barely disturbing the thick silence. The words felt like a betrayal, a reminder that the past was finally catching up with them. She took a step forward, her boots crunching on the gritty texture of spilled flour and plaster. The sound was like a crack in the silence, a small, jarring reminder that the world outside these walls still existed. Maureen didn't startle. She turned slowly, her face a mask of weary resignation. The crock was a dark, squat shape in her hands. “It needed to be fed,” she said, as if explaining something simple to a child. The words were laced with a deep sadness, a sense of loss that seemed to echo through the bakery. Elara Finch's eyes followed the movement of Maureen's hands, tracing the lines of her fingers as they cradled the crock. The gesture was almost tender, a reminder of the countless hours they had spent together, baking and laughing, their hands moving in perfect sync. “You killed him,” Elara Finch said. It wasn't a question. The words felt like a weight, a crushing burden that seemed to press down on her chest. She remembered the day Arthur Arthur Crofton died, the look of shock on his face, the sound of his wife's screams. It had been a sound that had haunted her ever since, a reminder of the darkness that lurked beneath the surface of their small town. The memory was like a cold draft, a chill that ran down her spine as she gazed at Maureen. A long sigh escaped Maureen, a sound that seemed to deflate her entire frame. She set the crock gently on the workbench, her fingers lingering on its cool glaze. The movement was almost imperceptible, but it was enough to make Elara Finch's heart ache. She remembered the countless hours they had spent together, baking and laughing, their hands moving in perfect sync. It was a memory that seemed to belong to another lifetime, a lifetime that was now lost forever. The air inside the bakery was thick with the scent of old bread and stale air. Elara Finch's nostrils flared, and she felt a pang of nostalgia wash over her. She remembered the smell of fresh dough, the way it filled the bakery and made her feel alive. A living monument.” She lifted it, not in threat, but in offering, towards Elara Finch. “It's hungry.” Elara Finch crossed the space and took the heavy crock. The ceramic was cold, but she imagined she could feel a faint, persistent warmth at its core, the slow, anaerobic breath of decades. She pried off the lid. The smell was profound—a deep, acidic, fruity tang, like apples and earth and time itself. The starter was healthy, a bubbly, beige-gray expanse with a thin layer of golden hooch on top. Alive.
