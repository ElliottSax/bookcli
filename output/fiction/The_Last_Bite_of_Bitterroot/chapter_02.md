Chapter 2: The Proof is in the Code

After midnight, silence in Arthur Crofton’s bakery transformed into something watchful, a stark contrast to its daytime hush. Under the green-glass banker’s lamp, stale air—a mix of old yeast and sharp industrial cleaner from the sheriff’s visit—hung over the scarred oak table. Elara Finch traced the looping cursive of his final newsletter, *The Bitterroot Rise*, her fingers leaving faint smudges of rye flour from her earlier fumbling in the pantry.
Staying had never been the plan. A nagging pull, which she’d blamed on the unfamiliar quiet of her rented cabin, drew her back. But standing in the dark, with moonlight laying cold rectangles across the floorboards, she caught it: the ghost of a recipe lingering in the air. Not a scent, but a structure. Arthur Crofton hadn’t just written a newsletter; he’d baked one.
On the surface, the prose was folksy and benign. ‘A Note on Hydration’ meandered through metaphors about mountain springs and the importance of a “slow, trusting merge” between water and flour. Yet the sentences carried a peculiar rhythm, a cadence that snagged her internal ear, trained over a decade to dissect menus and culinary manifestos.
“The water, clear as a guilty conscience, must be introduced with a patience this modern world forgets. Too fast, and you drown the grain’s true voice. Think of it as a confession, drawn out drop by drop, until the flour has no choice but to accept its fate and become something new.”

Professional curiosity curdled into something sharper. Reading on, her critic’s mind parsed the text for technique, not meaning. And there it was—a pattern. Every third sentence held an excess: a superfluous adjective, an oddly specific verb. *Golden syrup. Knead the truth. A burnt offering. The old growth.*

A slow, heavy thump began against her ribs. From her bag, she pulled a legal pad, its mundane yellow a stark contrast to the newsletter’s thick, cream-colored paper. Transcribing the outliers, her pen moved quickly.
*Knead the truth about the mayor’s golden syrup.*

Alone on the pad, the sentence stood absurd and menacing. This was no baking instruction. It was an accusation. Mayor Sterling, with his oil-slick smile and hands that looked like they’d never held anything heavier than a gavel. “Golden syrup” meant prosperity, sweetness, money—the very essence of the Skyline Resort development he championed, a sprawling complex of luxury cabins and a ski lift that would cut across the protected eastern ridge. Arthur Crofton had been quietly, fervently against it. Whispers at the general store and a faded ‘Preserve Bitterroot’ sticker on The bakery with its old brick oventhe estate’s back door had said as much.
Pushing back from the table, she sent the chair legs scraping like a gasp into the quiet. Pacing the small room, her eyes scanned the shelves of cookbooks, the jars of exotic seeds, a framed black-and-white photo of a younger, leaner Arthur Crofton standing before a massive, gnarled sequoia. An activist. A man who spoke in codes and sourdough starters. A man who might have known something worth killing for.
Context was needed. The newsletter referenced “past blends” and “foundation grains.” If this was a recipe, it was a multi-step one, built on history. The archives held the answers.
Turning to leave, her hand brushed the handle of her camera bag. She hesitated, then pulled out the camera itself. In the dim light, a divided second was all she took to focus on the last photograph Arthur Crofton ever took.
