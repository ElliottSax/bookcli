Chapter 7: The Bitter Harvest

The air in Margot's safehouse was thick with the smell of old paper, gun oil, and the ghost of a thousand cigarettes. It was a far cry from the gilded perfume of the Gala. Here, in a nondescript warehouse overlooking the river, the crew was unspooling, thread by thread.

Elara stood at a scarred workbench, the single, wilted orchid under a jeweller's loupe. Its purple was a bruise against the white steel. "It's not just the pesticide," she murmured, her voice husky with fatigue. "It's a signature. A cocktail. Paraquat, yes, but cut with something else... something floral, almost like orris root. It's been perfumed."

"A calling card," Leo said, hunched over a bank of glowing screens, his fingers a blur. He'd been diving into the digital sewers, the places where culinary obsession curdled into pathology. "And I think I've found the artist."

He swivelled a monitor. It displayed a mosaic of fragments: a blurred screenshot from a darknet forum, a grainy photo of a man in a wide-brimmed hat at a food market in Bangkok, a scan of a handwritten note praising the "song of a soon-to-be-extinct sea cucumber." The username linking them all was CuratorVeritas.

"The Curator," Leo announced. "Not a chef. A collector. His thing isn't just eating rare ingredients; it's possessing the last of them. The final bite of a species. The last harvest from a poisoned grove. He's a trophy hunter for the palate."

Margot, who had been silently cleaning a sleek, matte-black pistol, snapped the clip home with a definitive click. The sound echoed through the room, a stark punctuation mark on the crew's grim discussion. "I've heard the name," she said. "Whispered. He operates in the grey spaces between the gourmet markets and the outright criminal. He doesn't just buy; he commissions poaching. That orchid? It's from a protected cloud forest in Ecuador. The pesticide is his brand. He sterilizes the ground after he takes his prize. Ensures no one else ever tastes it again."

The revelation hung in the air, a toxic bloom. Elara felt a physical revulsion, a chef's nausea. Food was memory, connection, life. This was its inverse—a sacrament of endings.

"So our thief works for, or sells to, this monster," she concluded, her knuckles white on the bench's edge. "But the truffle wasn't at the Gala. The Curator didn't get it."

"No," Leo agreed, pulling up another window. "But he wanted it. Desperately. There's a bounty notice, placed three days before the Gala. Seven figures for 'Tuber magnatum pico, white Alba, of verified pedigree and unprecedented aroma.' The terms specified... live extraction."

"Live?" Elara asked.

"The thief had to steal the truffle and the tree's root system, the mycorrhizal network," Leo said, his face grim. "He doesn't just want the truffle; he wants the possibility of more. To own the source. The bounty went unclaimed. The auction winner was anonymous, but it wasn't him."

Margot stood, her movements fluid and predatory. "Then he'll be hungry. And men like him don't go to supermarkets. There's a place. An auction. Happens when the moon is dark. Ingredients that can't be found on any ledger. We need an invite."

Leo was already ahead of her, his code slithering into a server hosted in a jurisdiction that didn't exist. "I can forge credentials. But we need a buyer. Someone with the taste, the jargon, the... aura."

All eyes turned to Elara. She saw the reflection of the orchid's deathly purple in their expectant gazes. The chef in her recoiled. The accomplice stepped forward. "What's the cover?"

"A reclusive heiress," Margot said, a ghost of a smile on her lips. "Old money, new manias. Obsessed with culinary uniqueness. I'll be your security. Leo, our remote bidder and banker. We get in, we identify the Curator's agents, we find a trail."

"And we taste," Elara said, the words bitter on her tongue.

"You taste," Margot confirmed. "And you look like you belong."

Days passed, a blur of secrecy and preparation. The invitation arrived in the form of a customized business card, embossed with a naive watercolour of a forest glade and a handwritten note that read, "The moon dips low, the harvest lights shine bright." Tonight was the night. Tonight, they would mingle with the Curator's inner circle and peer into the abyss of the dark auction.

The crew arrived at the decommissioned hydraulic power station in its own sparkly sedan, a glint of black and steel that shed light on the silvered river, the rain-slicked handles of the old bridge rising like dying wings from the water. Margot, ever the strategist, had picked this spot for its undeniable charm and carefully scalpable periphery.

The auction was held in the belly of a decommissioned hydraulic power station, a cathedral of rust and brick. They arrived just before midnight, descending a slippery metal staircase into a cavernous space where the only light came from clinical LEDs illuminating glass display cases. The air was a riot of conflicting smells: the damp of the river, the sharp tang of ozone from a generator, and beneath it, the haunting, singular aromas of the world's rarest foods.

Elara, transformed in a simple but devastatingly cut black dress, her hair a severe knot, felt the eyes on her. Margot, in a tailored suit that couldn't hide the weapon at her spine, was a silent shadow. An attendant with dead eyes scanned their forged invitation and led them to a standing desk with a number: 13.

The other buyers were spectres. A woman in a sable coat, her face a mask of calculated boredom. A pair of Asian businessmen speaking in hushed Mandarin. A man with the hands of a surgeon, trembling slightly. And in the far corner, partially obscured by a massive, dripping block of glacial ice holding a king crab, was a figure in a long, dun-coloured duster, face hidden by the brim of a hat. The Curator? Or another ghoul?

A man in a dinner jacket, his voice a cultured monotone, took a small podium. "Lot One. Densuke watermelon. From a single, secret plot in Hokkaido. This year's yield: six fruits. This is the finest. The sugar content is a record. Opening bid: twenty thousand."

The bidding was silent, conducted via discreet tablets. Leo's voice was a whisper in Elara's concealed earpiece. "Number seven is pushing. The sable lady. She's legit—owns a pharmaceutical chain. Bidding for status, not love."

Elara watched the numbers climb, feeling sick. A watermelon. It was just a watermelon.
