# Chapter 8: The Second Mover

The confession hung in the air of the interview room like a chemical smoke, acrid and slow to dissipate. Sheriff Briggs, reduced to a man in a too-large chair, had finished speaking. The tape recorder’s spools had stopped turning, their faint mechanical click echoing in the sudden silence. Elara watched him, not with the fury she expected, but with a profound, weary pity. His lie had been a stone dropped in the pond of her investigation; now she had to trace the ripples to their source, each concentric circle leading further from the center he’d tried to protect.
“This Leo,” she said, her voice scraping the silence like a match striking. “You never questioned him. Not properly.”

Briggs wouldn’t meet her eyes. He studied his own hands, knotted together on the table—hands that had once seemed capable of holding the whole town’s order, now just flesh and bone and regret. The fluorescent light buzzed overhead, casting a sickly green pallor on his skin. “He was a kid. Shook up. Said he’d brought Elias his evening tea at seven, found the study door locked. Called through, got no answer. Came straight to me at the station.” He paused, his throat working. “We went back together, broke the door in.” He recited it like a catechism he no longer believed, the words worn smooth and meaningless from repetition. “His story was simple. It fit. And I… I had my own panic to manage. My own guilt. Leo was just part of the scenery.”

“A person,” Elara corrected, coldly, the word a deliberate weight. “A young man who lived in the house, who had access, who found the body with you, and then vanished days after the funeral. Where did he go?”

“No one knows.” Briggs’s gaze drifted to the room’s single, wire-reinforced window, as if looking for the ghost of that departure. “He wasn’t from here. Drifted in a few years before, quiet, good with his hands. Elias took him on as a sort of live-in handyman and assistant. Replied he saw ‘potential’ in him. Leo kept to the old servant quarters in the east wing. After the funeral, he simply packed a bag and was gone. I didn’t stop him.” He finally looked up, his eyes haunted by the specter of his own negligence. “I was almost glad. One less thread to worry about. One less pair of eyes that had seen… what was in that room.”

Elara pushed back from the table, the chair legs shrieking on the linoleum—a sound like a small animal in distress. The atmospheric pressure in the room had shifted; the claustrophobia was no longer about the small space, but about the narrowing tunnel of truth. Someone had moved after the murder. A second mover. The killer had used the secret ways, but someone else had perhaps locked the study door from the outside, creating the sealed-room illusion before the sheriff arrived. Someone with opportunity in that narrow, terrible window between death and discovery. A collaborator? An opportunist? Or a witness sealing the scene for their own reasons?
“I need access to his room,” she said, the statement leaving no room for debate.
Briggs nodded, a defeated gesture that appeared to start at his shoulders and sink through his entire body. “The keys are at the station. I’ll have Deputy Miller meet you there. It’s… it’s been untouched, far as I know. The door swung inward on complaining hinges, releasing a breath of still, frigid air. Leo’s room was a monk’s cell, ten feet by twelve. A single iron-framed bed, stripped bare, a thin mattress stained with Rorschach blots of damp and time. A plain wardrobe of cheap pine, a small desk and chair positioned beneath a grimy window that looked out onto the dense, encroaching woods—a view of green imprisonment. The air was stale, carrying the layered scents of old wood, dust, and something faintly chemical—turpentine, perhaps, or fixative. The smell of art.
Elara started methodically, her forensic training a welcome algorithm against the swelling tide of intuition. She checked under the bed, ran her fingers along the tops of the door frames, her fingertips coming away gray with undisturbed dust. A section about two feet square seemed slightly different—the paper was newer, less stained by time and moisture, and the edges were too perfect, too straight.
