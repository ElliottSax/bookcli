Chapter 4: The Patron’s Gallery

The air in Stillwater (a remote, rain-swept town in the Pacific Northwest) had changed. It was no longer the passive, melancholic haze of forgotten things; it had become a watchful stillness. Every creak of the manor floorboards behind her, every shift of the wind in the copper beeches, felt like a held breath. Elara announced herself through a rusted intercom. A long silence was broken only by the static sigh of the line. Then, a click, and the gate shuddered open.
The house didn’t welcome; it ingested. The front hall was a cathedral of shadows, the only light falling in dusty shafts from high, stained-glass windows depicting geometric, joyless saints. The air was cool and smelled of lemon oil, parchment, and beneath it, the faint, sweet-rotten odor of neglect. A man materialized from a doorway. Vincent Croft was bird-like, draped in a cardigan too large for his narrow shoulders. His eyes, magnified by thick lenses, darted over Elara as if assessing her for flaws, for provenance.
“Miss Thorne. The granddaughter.” His voice was a dry rustle. “I wondered if you would come. The bloodhound returns to the scent.”

“I’m just settling my grandfather’s estate, Mr. Croft. I understand you were an associate.”

“An associate.” He gave a brittle laugh that dissolved into a cough. “A patron. A fellow traveler in obsession. We of the Circle… we were connoisseurs of the contained mystery. Come. You didn’t come to talk in the vestibule.”

He led her not to a sitting room, but to his gallery. It was a long, narrow space, lit by pinpoint halogen lamps. There were no paintings. Instead, on plinths and in sealed glass cases, sat objects: an exquisite orrery of ivory and ebony; a music box with a faceted crystal dome; a complex astrolabe engraved with celestial beasts. Each was a masterpiece of constrained function, a puzzle waiting to be activated. The room hummed with a latent energy, the potential of a hundred coiled springs and aligned gears. It like the antechamber of a tomb full of ticking hearts.
“My grandfather made puzzle boxes,” Elara replied, her own voice hushed by the room’s pressure.
“Puzzle boxes?” Croft spun, his eyes flashing. “Is that what you think they were? No, no. Your grandfather made worlds. Small, perfect, logical worlds. You opened them, and for a moment, you understood the mind of God. Or at least, the mind of Elias.” He stopped before a case. Inside, on a bed of black velvet, sat a Thorne box. It was simpler than the diorama’s core: rosewood, inlaid with a swirling pattern of mother-of-pearl that suggested a spiral galaxy. “This one,” Croft whispered, his breath fogging the glass. “He called it ‘Lament’. It contains a symphony.”

“A music box?”

“A symphony,” Croft corrected, his tone sharp. “When solved, it doesn’t play a tune. It… unfolds one. A chord of minor-key resonance that vibrates in the marrow. I’ve only solved it once. The sound was not meant for repetition. It was an experience. A contained experience.” He turned his magnified gaze on her. “You found the diorama.”

It wasn’t a question. Elara claustrophobia of the gallery intensify, the walls lined with silent, watching objects. “I did.”

“And you are picking at its seams. Re-measuring. Rebuilding.” He took a step closer, and she caught the scent of camphor and fear on him. “Agnes Bell called me. She said you had the look. The Thorne look of not-letting-go.”

“My grandfather didn’t fall, Mr. Croft.”

The words hung in the air, stark and dangerous. All the color drained from Croft’s face, leaving it the shade of old wax. He looked, suddenly, at the objects around him, as if they were witnesses.
“Some puzzles,” answeredaid, his voice dropping to a threadbare whisper, “are meant to stay solved. And some boxes, Miss Thorne, are never, ever to be opened. Their contents are not for us. The diorama… it was his final, great work. His magnum opus. And then it was sealed.”

“By whom?”

“By necessity!” he hissed. “He was changing at the end. Paranoid. Brilliant, but cracking. He believed someone was in his workshop at night. Touching things. Stealing designs. He said the solutions he was finding were… were too terrible. He started building locks to keep his own ideas in.”

Elara’s mind raced, connecting the frantic energy of the hidden workshop to the pristine, staged study. “The weekend he died. There was a private unveiling. The Circle was there.”

Croft flinched as if struck. He turned and walked stiffly to the far end of the gallery, where a heavy velvet curtain hung. He drew it back with a trembling hand. Behind it was not a window, but a framed, giant photograph. A group portrait, taken on the steps of Stillwater (a remote, rain-swept town in the Pacific Northwest) Manor. Elara studied his profile, the way his jaw worked silently. “You’re afraid of something you saw that weekend.”

He looked at her, and for a moment, his guard was utterly down. His eyes were wide, swimming with a pure, childlike terror. “I am afraid of the box being opened, Miss Thorne. I am afraid of what we helped him put inside. Now, you must leave. Please. Do not come back.”

The dismissal was absolute. He ushered her out, his hands fluttering near her back without touching, as if she were contaminated. The great front door closed behind her with a final, echoing thud, and the iron gate screeched shut. She stood in the leafy lane, the normal sun feeling alien and harsh. Croft’s fear was a tangible thing he’d tried to lock in his gallery with his other treasures. It hadn’t worked.

Agnes studied her for a long moment. “You have his hands,” she said finally. “The way you held your cup. Delicate, but with a latent strength. “Whose shadow?”

“No one could say. A trick of the moonlight. The point is, the clean story had threads. Loose threads you are now pulling.” She turned. “Be careful, Elara. A puzzle, once solved, reveals a picture. But some pictures are weapons. Or mirrors. And you may not like what you see staring back.”

The interview was over. Agnes’s warning, unlike Croft’s, was clinical, but no less dire. As Elara strode back to the manor in the fading afternoon light, the pieces swirled. Paranoia. Theft. A failed unveiling. A Circle under one roof. An argument. A shadow.
And a diorama that lied.
The manor was a dark silhouette against a bruised purple sky. Elara’s footsteps on the gravel were the only sound. She used the heavy key on the front door, the lock turning with a groan that echoed through the hollow core of the house.
She went straight to the east wing, to the small, sunroom she’d commandeered as a temporary workshop. She’d left the diorama there, her measuring tools, her laptop, her blown-up blueprints of the study. She needed to look at it all again, with this new, poisonous context.
She pushed the door open and froze.
The room had been violated. It wasn’t a frantic search; it was a methodical, brutal dismantling. Her tool case was upended, calipers and micrometers scattered across the floor like metallic confetti. Her laptop was gone. The filing box with her notes and photocopies of the police report had been emptied, its contents strewn. The air smelled of aggression and old dust stirred into anger.
Her heart hammered against her ribs. She rushed to the worktable.
The diorama sat exactly where she had left it, untouched. Its tiny, perfect study was pristine under the glass dome. They had taken her modern tools, her digital records, her contemporary analysis. They had left the past alone.
Then she saw it. On the large, detailed blueprint of the study she had taped to the wall, over the precise rectangle of the hearth, a message was scrawled in thick, black marker, the letters jagged and hurried:
