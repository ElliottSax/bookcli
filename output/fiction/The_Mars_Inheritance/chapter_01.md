The city's constant noise - the hum of mag-lev transports, the wail of sirens, and the murmur of comms devices - created a steady background din. From her fourteenth-floor apartment, Maya Blackwood watched as people converged on transit hubs, their lives following a predictable routine. The air in her small apartment was stale, thick with the smell of yesterday's reheated synth-noodles and the ozone tang of overworked electronics. She held a chipped ceramic mug, and the steam from her coffee condensed on the cool windowpane, tracing fleeting patterns that mirrored the turmoil in her thoughts.
It had been a year since her father, Alistair Blackwood, died. Three hundred and sixty-five days of silence where she should have heard his voice. Her father had been a quiet, intense man. His study, with its leather-bound books and scattered data-slates, still like a sanctuary, though the comforting smell of his pipe tobacco was gone. The official story was that he died in a lab accident - a catastrophic containment failure. But the report appeared too slick, too polished. Maya thought of it as a void that had swallowed her father, and now it was swallowing her.
As a journalist for the Olympus Prime Chronicle, Maya had tried to find out what really happened. She filed FOIA requests, cultivated sources, and scrutinized public logs. But every lead ended in a dead end, a wall of bureaucratic stone. Her days were filled with empty stories - city council disputes, tech expos. The unanswered questions hung over her, a constant, nagging presence that drove her to keep searching. She just didn't know where to look yet.
A sharp beep from her comms device broke the silence. It was an old, clunky model that her father had once called "secure." Few people had the frequency.
