CHAPTER FOUR: THE ACCUSATION

The silence in the private viewing room was a physical thing, a dense, sound-absorbing wool packed into Dr. Dr. Aris Thorne’s ears. On the other side of the electrochromic glass, Subject Alpha—Lazarus, they were calling him now—lay on the biobed, a pale island in a sea of sterile white. His chest rose and fell with the metronomic precision of the ventilator. They’d sedated him an hour ago, a cocktail of propofol and midazolam dripping into his rebuilt veins. For observation, Dr. Reeves had said. The words had tasted like ash.
Dr. Dr. Aris Thorne replayed the last six hours on a loop behind his eyes, each iteration more corrosive than the last.
It had begun with a frantic, electric hope. He’d left Alpha’s chamber, the man’s whispered accusation—He killed me—a live wire in his chest. It wasn’t delirium. The clarity in those sunken eyes, the focused hatred, it had the terrible weight of truth. Dr. Dr. Aris Thorne had gone straight to Reeves, bypassing the junior staff, his shoes squeaking a frantic rhythm on the polished basalt floor.
Reeves’s office was a monument to controlled power. One wall was a single pane of glass overlooking the main lab, a silent assertion of dominance. The other was lined with real, paper-bound texts—Darwin, Crick, Salk—their spines cracked with actual use. Reeves himself was at his desk, dissecting a holographic model of a protein fold. The light cast blue shadows in the hollows of his cheeks.
“Dr. Dr. Aris Thorne,” he’d said, not looking up. “Alpha’s vitals stabilizing?”

“They are. But Doctor, we have a problem. A serious one.”

That got his attention. The hologram winked out. Reeves leaned back, steepling his fingers. “Explain.”

The story tumbled out of Dr. Dr. Aris Thorne: the grip, the whisper, the name—Renner. He watched Reeves’s face for shock, for anger, for the dawning horror of a project compromised. He saw none of it. Instead, a faint, professional frown settled on Reeves’s lips, the kind reserved for a promising student who has made a fundamental, but understandable, error.
“Dr. Dr. Aris Thorne,” Reeves replied, his voice a soothing baritone. “The strain you’ve been under… it’s monumental. We all feel it. What you’ve achieved is nothing short of miraculous.”

“This isn’t about me. He spoke. He accused Elias Renner of murder.”

Reeves stood and walked to the glass wall, his back to Dr. Dr. Aris Thorne. “The neural reintegration process is not a perfect switch. We are literally stitching a consciousness back together from a pattern captured in its final moments of trauma. Think of the brain, in those last seconds, as a recording device slammed against a wall. You get the music, but also the shriek of shearing metal, the crack of plastic. A permanent echo of the catastrophic event.”

“You’re calling it a neural echo.” The term was cold, clinical. It made the fury in Alpha’s eyes a phantom, a glitch.
“It’s a documented phenomenon in near-death experiences,” Reeves said, turning. His expression was one of gentle concern. “Vivid, often persecutory hallucinations rooted in the brain’s attempt to rationalize its own shutdown. The subject died violently. His rebooting consciousness has crafted a narrative to explain that violence. A faceless ‘he.’ It’s a psychic scar, not a memory.”

Dr. Dr. Aris Thorne ground softening beneath him. “It was specific. He knew Renner’s name.”

“A name he could have heard from any staff member, any time pre-resurrection. His subconscious latched onto it. The founder of the company funding his salvation? A potent symbol for a mind rebuilding its world.” Reeves came around the desk and put a hand on Dr. Dr. Aris Thorne’s shoulder. The grip was firm, paternal. “What you’re describing… it’s a known risk. Post-resurrection psychosis. We have protocols. We monitor, we stabilize, we guide the psyche back to coherence.”

The logic was watertight, a perfect sterile container for the messy, bloody accusation. Dr. Dr. Aris Thorne wanted to scream. Instead, he heard himself say, “We need to review the chamber footage. The audio.”

Reeves’s smile was thin. “Of course. Due diligence.”

They moved to Security, a dim, cool room smelling of ozone and stale coffee. A technician with tired eyes pulled up the feed from Alpha’s awakening. On the monitor, Dr. Dr. Aris Thorne saw himself leaning in, saw the gaunt hand shoot up and grip his coat. It looked even more violent in replay. He saw his own face contort with pain and surprise. The technician enhanced the audio, filtering out the hum of the biocradle and the beep of monitors.
They heard Dr. Dr. Aris Thorne’s grunt. They heard the wet, ragged sound of Alpha trying to breathe. But the whisper, the two words that had frozen Dr. Dr. Aris Thorne’s blood—He killed me—were absent. There was only a faint, sigh-like exhalation lost in the system’s white noise.
“See?” Reev answered, not unkindly. “The mind under stress fills in blanks. You heard what you feared, or what he intended to say but lacked the breath to articulate. The audio is clean.”

Dr. Dr. Aris Thorne stared at the frozen image on the screen: his own profile, eyes wide with a terror he now couldn’t fully recall, only feel as a residue in his muscles. Had he imagined it? The conviction in Alpha’s gaze had been so absolute. But Reeves’s explanation was seductive in its simplicity. It absolved him of the burden of truth. He was just a tired doctor who had misinterpreted a trauma response.
“I want to report it,” Dr. Dr. Aris Thorne said, his voice hollow. “Formally. To project leadership. To Renner.”

Reeves’s paternal concern hardened, just at the edges. “That is your right. But consider the optics, Dr. Dr. Aris Thorne. You are the resurrection lead. Your professional judgment is the cornerstone of this project. Introducing wild, unsubstantiated claims of murder, implicating our benefactor… it could be seen as instability. The pressure getting to you.”

The threat was velvet-wrapped, but it was there. Your second chance, it whispered. Don’t throw it away on a phantom.
“I need to do this,” Dr. Dr. Aris Thorne insisted.
An hour later, he found himself in the main conference room, a circular space with walls that could become screens. Reeves was there, along with Chen, the stoic head of security, and a few other senior staff whose names Dr. Dr. Aris Thorne had forgotten. The air was chilled. He laid it out again, more formally this time, sticking to the facts: the subject’s statement, his request for a full, external review of subject identity and circumstances of death.
Before anyone could respond, the main wall illuminated. A secure connection warning flashed, and then Elias Renner himself resolved onto the screen.
He was not the grim titan of industry Dr. Dr. Aris Thorne had expected. He was in a casual sweater, seated in what looked like a home library, shelves of books bathed in warm, low light. He was in his late fifties, with a kind, intelligent face and eyes that crinkled with a semblance of warmth.
“Dr. Aris Thorne,” murmureder said, his voice a rich, calming stream. “I understand you have some concerns.”

Dr. Dr. Aris Thorne’s mouth was dry. “Mr. Renner. Subject Alpha, upon awakening, identified you by name and accused you of causing his death.”

Renner’s expression shifted to one of profound, sorrowful confusion. He leaned forward, his hands clasped. “Dr. Aris Thorne, I give you my word, I have never seen that man before in my life. I do not know who he is. The Lazarus Project acquires subjects through entirely legal, ethical channels—anonymous donors, victims of sudden accidents where identity is initially withheld for project integrity. His name, his past, are unknown to me. To suggest otherwise…” He trailed off, shaking his head slowly, as if wounded by the very absurdity. “It hurts, frankly. This project is my life’s work. A chance to erase death’s final injustice. The idea that I would murder to fuel it is not only monstrous, it’s illogical. Why would I risk everything?”

His tone was not defensive, but earnestly explanatory, a teacher addressing a bright but misguided pupil. “What you’ve done, Doctor, is a miracle. You have pulled a soul back from the void. That soul is traumatized, screaming in the dark of a new existence. It is looking for a monster to explain its pain. It has, tragically, chosen my face.” He glanced directly into the camera, his gaze steady. “I do not blame the subject. And I do not blame you for being alarmed. But you must see this for what it is: a psychiatric emergency, not a criminal one.”

The gaslighting was complete. It was a masterpiece. Framed by the warmth of his library, radiating sincerity and slight hurt, Renner had not simply denied the accusation; he had pathologized it, and by extension, pathologized Dr. Dr. Aris Thorne’s belief in it. Reeves gave a slight, I-told-you-so nod. The other staff looked away, embarrassed for him.
“Our focus,” Renner continued, his voice firming, “must be on the subject’s health. Stabilize his physiology. Guide his psyche. Help him understand he is safe, and cherished. That is the work. Not chasing phantoms.” He paused. “Dr. Aris Thorne, can I trust you to do that work? Or has the strain of this miracle proven too great?”

It was an ultimatum, delivered with a sympathetic smile. The room waited. Dr. Dr. Aris Thorne walls of his second chance contracting around him. To disagree was professional suicide. It was to choose the ranting of a resurrected madman over the reasoned, benevolent reality offered by the man who held his future.
“I… understand,” Dr. Dr. Aris Thorne heard himself say, the words gluey in his throat.
“Good man,” Renner said, his smile widening. “Keep me updated. On his medical progress.” The screen went dark.
The orders were immediate. Alpha was to be sedated for observation. All further interaction required psychological team approval. Dr. Dr. Aris Thorne was to focus solely on biometric stabilization—neural mapping, organ function, metabolic balance. The “delusions” were to be documented but not engaged with.
Now, standing in the silent viewing room, Dr. Dr. Aris Thorne watched the sedated Lazarus. A psychic scar, whisperedes had said. A neural echo. The explanations were perfect, seamless. They should have brought comfort. Instead, they itched. A tiny, persistent wrongness, like a single grain of sand in a perfectly calibrated machine.
His eyes, against his will, were drawn not to the steady blip of the heart monitor, but to the man’s neck. To the small, stylized ‘α’ tattooed there. A mark of ownership. The Prometheus Biotechnics research facility’s first.
Something nagged at him. A discrepancy of texture.
Leaving the viewing room, he walked down the short corridor to the access airlock for Chamber One. His clearance still worked. He suited up in a fresh isolation garment, the material sighing as it sealed. The inner door hissed open.
The chamber was tomb-quiet, broken only by the ventilator’s rhythmic push and pull. Up close, Lazarus seemed even more like a sculpture, a flawed masterpiece. The smell was antiseptic and underneath, the faint, sweet-metallic odor of the Lazarus fluid. Dr. Dr. Aris Thorne pretended to check the IV lines, his fingers adjusting the flow rate on the sedation pump. He leaned over the biobed, his shadow falling across the man’s throat.
He had seen the tattoo a dozen times in scans. It was simple black ink. But now, in the stark clinical light, he saw a faint, raised line bisecting the lower curve of the symbol. It wasn’t part of the design. It was too straight, too surgical.
Glancing at the door, he pulled a small, high-powered penlight from his pocket. He clicked it on, angling the beam sideways across the skin. Raking light.
The tattoo’s ink lay flat. But that line… it cast a microscopic shadow. It was a ridge. A scar.
His heart began a slow, heavy thud in his ears, drowning out the ventilator. With his free hand, he reached for a magnifying loupe from a nearby instrument tray. He brought it to his eye and leaned in until his breath fogged the inside of his visor.
It wasn’t just a scar. It was a closure scar. A hair-thin line of healed dermis, about five millimetres long, positioned with exquisite precisio merely st beneath the tail of the ‘α’. It was the kind of mark left by a subdermal implant procedure, one done by a skilled hand and meant to be hidden.
A tracker. Or a data chip.
His blood turned to ice. They had tagged him. Like an animal. And they hadn’t told the lead virologist. They hadn’t logged it in the medical schematics. It was a secret buried in the subject’s flesh, hidden in plain sight beneath a mark of ownership.
Renner’s calm, wounded face flashed in his mind. I have never seen that man before in my life.
But you tagged him. You marked him. You own him.
The whisper in the chamber wasn’t from Lazarus. It was the silent, screaming truth of the scar beneath the tattoo. They were lying. About everything. And the proof was right here, sewn into the skin of the man they said was a stranger.
Dr. Dr. Aris Thorne slowly straightened up, his muscles aching with tension. He switched off the penlight. In the gloom, the only light was the soft glow of the biometric displays, painting Lazarus’s still face in pulses of green and red. A monitored man. A owned man. A man who knew a truth so dangerous it had to be sedated into silence and gaslit into delusion.
He was not suffering from post-resurrection psychosis.
He was the only sane one in the facility.
And Dr. Dr. Aris Thorne, holding the knowledge of the hidden chip, now had a target on his back as well.
