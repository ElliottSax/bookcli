Chapter 1: The Fallen Man

Rain didn’t fall on Berlin so much as atomize, hurled against the city by a spiteful wind. A fine, grey grit seeped through the seams of coats and the resolve of pedestrians. From his third-floor apartment window, Dr. Aris Thorne traced the rivulets on the pane with a fingertip, numb from the chill leaching through the glass. Outside, the world smeared into a watercolour of grey concrete and sodium-orange streetlights, a perfect match for the interior landscape of his mind.
His apartment was a study in absence. A single, half-empty bookshelf held outdated virology textbooks and paperback thrillers with broken spines. A sofa the colour of dust. In the functional kitchenette, the most complex organism cultivated was penicillin on forgotten bread. The only sign of his former life was a framed photograph on the bare desk: a younger Aris, grinning beside a gleaming PCR machine, his arm around a woman with intelligent eyes and a laugh that had once like sunlight. Lena. He never looked at it directly, only absorbed its presence in his periphery—a persistent ghost of a self he could no longer access.
Memory of the fall was a physical thing, a shard of ice permanently lodged behind his sternum. It didn’t come as a narrative, but as sensory flashes, brutal and disjointed. The blinding, sterile white of a conference hall in Geneva. A thousand eyes upon him, the heat palpable. The sharp, sour smell of his own sweat under a bespoke suit. The microphone, a cold, metallic cylinder in his hand, amplified every tremor.
“The data from the Jakarta strain… the cross-species virulence was… miscalculated.”

*Miscalculated.* The word echoed, pathetic and small.
Then the stones hurled from the audience: “Fabrication.” “Negligence.” “Catastrophic oversight.”

Professor Halstrom, his senior collaborator, simply turned his chair away. The dismissal was absolute, louder than any shout. Then he found Lena’s face in the crowd. She wasn’t angry. She was hollow, erased by a disappointment so complete it stole his breath.
The freefall was swift. Retracted papers. Revoked grants. The quiet, administrative euthanasia of a career. He hadn’t falsified data. He’d been brilliant, ambitious, and catastrophically wrong. In the world of pandemic prevention, wrong was a sin with its own special circle of hell.
Turning from the window, the silence in the apartment pressed in, a low hum in the inner ear. He moved to the small desk and powered on his aging laptop. His current work was a digital purgatory: freelance copy-editing for obscure biomedical journals, checking the grammar of breakthroughs he would never be part of. The screen’s blue glow was the only colour in the room.
The buzzer from the street door shocked the silence, a jagged sound tearing the fabric of his isolation. Visitors never came. Delivery men left packages in the foyer. He let it ring, hoping for a mistake.
It rang again. Insistently. Three short, precise bursts.
Cautious, he went to the intercom. “Yes?”

“Dr. Aris Thorne?” A woman’s voice, cool and modulated, cut through the static with effortless clarity. “My name is Elara Vance. I represent The Prometheus Biotechnics research facility. I’d appreciate a moment of your time.”

*The Prometheus Biotechnics research facility.* The name sent a faint, almost forgotten current through him. A biotech phantom. Whispered about at the fringes of legitimate conferences, a black hole of private funding that swallowed top-tier researchers and produced no public papers. A myth, or a graveyard.
“I’m not interested in whatever you’re selling,” Aris said, his voice rougher than intended.
“I’m not selling anything, Doctor. I’m buying. Your expertise. Five minutes.”

Authority brooked no argument in her tone, a confidence belonging to people who navigated the world without apology—the antithesis of his own shambling uncertainty. Against every instinct screaming at him to retreat, his thumb pressed the door release. The lock disengaged with a heavy, metallic clunk that like a point of no return.
Her footsteps on the stairs were measured, even taps that spoke of expensive, practical footwear—not the hurried clatter of a courier. A knock, firm but not loud.
He opened the door.
Elara Vance was both exactly and nothing like he’d imagined. Perhaps forty, she possessed a poised, ageless quality. Hair the colour of dark honey was pulled into a severe but elegant knot. She wore a tailored charcoal coat, unbuttoned, over a simple black ensemble. No jewellery save for a sleek, matte-black watch. Her eyes were her most striking feature: a pale, crystalline grey that appeared to absorb the dim hallway light and reflect it back, calculated and cool. One sweeping, unhurried glance assessed him and the apartment, betraying no reaction to his worn sweater or the monastic bleakness of his space.
“Dr. Aris Thorne,” she said, not offering a hand. “May I?”

He stepped aside, wordlessly.
She entered as if taking possession of a room she’d already purchased, her presence immediately expanding to fill the emptiness. Standing near the centre of the room, she became a pillar of quiet intent.
“You live quietly,” she observed, not unkindly. A statement of fact.

Aris flinched. The casual mention of Jakarta was a needle slipped between his ribs.
