 <s> [OUTST] The air in the lab was thick with the scent of ozone and the low hum of machinery. Dr. McClure adjusted the settings on the fMRI machine, his fingers dancing over the controls with practiced ease. The participant, a young man in his early twenties, lay still on the table, his head secured in the scanner. The screen flickered to life, displaying a series of images designed to trigger different decision-making pathways in the brain.

“Ready?” Dr. McClure asked, his voice calm and steady.

The young man nodded, his eyes fixed on the screen. The first image appeared—a stack of cash, crisp and new. The ventral striatum in his brain lit up like a Christmas tree, the neural activity spiking as the promise of immediate reward flooded his system.

“Now, the next one,” Dr. McClure instructed, his voice barely above a whisper.

The image changed to a distant future scenario—a retirement account, a house, a family. The activity shifted, the prefrontal cortex engaging as the brain struggled to weigh the abstract benefits of delayed gratification against the visceral pull of the present.

Dr. McClure watched the data scroll across the screen, his expression thoughtful. “Fascinating,” he murmured. “The neural competition is clear. The limbic system is fighting for control, while the prefrontal cortex is trying to assert itself. It’s a battle between impulse and reason.”

The young man’s breath hitched as the images cycled through, each one a test of his willpower. The scent of antiseptic and the sterile white walls of the lab did little to distract from the intensity of the moment. He could feel the tension in his muscles, the way his heart rate quickened with each decision.

“How does it feel?” Dr. McClure asked, his voice gentle.

“Hard,” the young man admitted, his voice tight. “It’s like my brain is split in two. One part wants the money now, the other knows it’s better to wait.”

Dr. McClure nodded, his eyes never leaving the screen. “That’s the essence of present bias. The immediate reward is so compelling, so tangible, that it’s hard to resist. But the key is to find a way to make the future rewards just as real, just as tangible.”

The young man exhaled slowly, his mind racing. He thought about his goals, his dreams, the life he wanted to build. It was easy to get lost in the moment, to let the immediate gratification cloud his judgment. But he knew, deep down, that the choices he made today would shape his future.

“What if I could see myself in the future?” he asked, his voice hopeful. “Would that help?”

Dr. McClure smiled, a glimmer of pride in his eyes. “That’s exactly what we’re working on. Making the future self more tangible, more real. Because when you can see the person you’re making these decisions for, it changes everything.”

The young man nodded, a sense of determination settling over him. He knew the road ahead wouldn’t be easy, but he was ready to fight for his future. One decision at a time.

The conference room was a symphony of muted tones and soft light. The walls were adorned with charts and graphs, each one proof of the power of present bias. Dr. Hershfield stood at the front of the room, his voice steady and measured as he addressed the gathered researchers.

“Ladies and gentlemen, we’ve all seen the data. The human brain is wired to prioritize immediate rewards, often at the expense of long-term consequences. But what if we could change that? What if we could make the future self more tangible, more real?”

He clicked a button, and the screen behind him flickered to life. A virtual reality avatar of an older man appeared, his face lined with age and experience. The room fell silent as the researchers leaned in, their eyes fixed on the screen.

“This is what we’re working towards,” Dr. Hershfield continued. “A tool that allows individuals to see themselves in the future, to make the abstract concept of the future self more concrete. Because when you can see the person you’re making these decisions for, it changes everything.”

The researchers murmured among themselves, their minds racing with possibilities. They thought about the implications, the ways this technology could be used to help people make better decisions, to align their actions with their long-term goals.

Dr. Hershfield smiled, a sense of satisfaction washing over him. He knew the road ahead wouldn’t be easy, but he was ready to fight for the future. One decision at a time.
