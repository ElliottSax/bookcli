The lecture hall was a cavernous space, its walls lined with rows of worn wooden chairs, their seats creaking softly as the students shifted in their seats. The air was cool and carried the faint, clean scent of lemon polish from the recently wiped-down desks, undercut by the mineral smell of chilled air from the vents overhead. On the massive screen behind Dr. Aris Thorne, a single statistic glowed with sterile finality: 73%. A 2023 study at the Stanford Graduate School of Business had found that nearly three-quarters of corporate strategy decisions could be reversed simply by altering the verbal description of the options, while keeping the objective outcomes numerically identical.

Thorne stood, his movement fluid and deliberate, and walked to the side of the stage, his shadow stretching long and thin across the screen. He stood there for a moment, his eyes scanning the room, taking in the sea of expectant faces. He could see the questions in their eyes, the unspoken doubts and the dawning realizations. And he knew that, in this moment, he had given them something valuable, something that would stay with them long after the lecture was over.

He took a deep breath, the scent of the lemon polish and the mineral smell of the air conditioning filling his lungs.
