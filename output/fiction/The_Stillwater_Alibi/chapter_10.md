Chapter 10: The Librarian’s Truth

The rain had settled into a permanent state of being, a fine, cold mist that seeped through the very stones of Stillwater. It beaded on the window of Eleanor Vance’s hospital room, distorting the view of the sodden The town square with cobblestones and Victorian architecture into a watercolour of grey and gloom. The air smelled of antiseptic and wilted lilies from a get-well bouquet that already seemed part of the decay.
Eleanor lay propped against stark white pillows, her face the colour of parchment. A bandage wrapped her head, disappearing into her usually precise bun, now a frayed nest of grey. Her glasses were on the bedside table, and without them, her eyes looked small, defenceless, and swimming with a pain that went deeper than the concussion.
Detective Elias Crowe sat beside the bed, a notebook open but untouched on his knee. He’d been silent for a full five minutes, letting the rhythmic hiss-click of the IV pump fill the room. He’d learned that in places like this, silence was the sharpest tool. It pried things open.
“He looked like her,” Eleanor whispered, her voice raspy from disuse and the tube they’d removed. She wasn’t looking at Elias Crowe, but at the water-streaked window. “Around the eyes. The set of the jaw. Lillian Silas Thorne. My aunt.”

Elias Crowe didn’t move. “Lillian Silas Thorne died in the fire. 1947.”

“No,” Eleanor said, the word a soft exhalation. “She lived. She was sent away to a convent in disgrace, pregnant. The family story was that she’d perished, a tragic accident. It was cleaner. My mother was her younger sister. She knew. She told me, before she died, as a secret to be kept. A family shame.”

The pieces, jagged and corroded, slide into a different, uglier configuration. The 1947 fire wasn’t just a cover for a theft, as Samuel had implied; it was a pyre for a reputation.
“The child?” Elias Crowe asked.
“A boy. Adopted by a family in Portland, records sealed. The Silas Thorne money ensured that.” Eleanor finally turned her head, her gaze finding Elias Crowe’s. It was full of a profound, weary guilt. “William Silas Thorne—the man who came here—was that boy. My cousin. I knew the moment I saw his reader’s card application. The name was different, but the date of birth, the place… and those eyes. I’d seen them in our portrait gallery, gathering dust.”

“You didn’t approach him.”

“How could I?” A tear escaped, tracing a path through the fine lines on her temple. “Welcome to Stillwater, we burned your mother’s legacy and hid you like a dirty secret? He was researching the town, the Stillwater Clock Tower (central landmark), the fire. He was a historian. He was piecing it together himself. He’d come home.”

The atmosphere in the room grew claustrophobic, the walls pressing in with the weight of generations of secrets. The mist outside appeared to thicken, swallowing the world beyond the glass.
“The key, Eleanor,” Elias Crowe said, his voice low but inexorable. “You replied it was on your person all night.”

She flinched as if struck. “I lied. The night he died… he came to the library just before closing. He was excited. Her hands, lying on the starch-stiff sheet, trembled. Elias Crowe waited.

Elias Crowe’s mind raced, tripping over the impossibility. A locked room. A key inside. A third voice.
“I panicked. I started pounding on the door, calling his name. Nothing. Then I heard it.” Her voice dropped to a haunted whisper. “From deep inside the walls, a grinding, a whirring of ancient gears. Elias Crowe sat back, the plastic chair creaking under his weight. The puzzle box had been shaken, and the pieces inside now revealed a different, more sinister picture. The key in the lock. The third voice. The machinery.
“The killer didn’t leave through the door,” he said, thinking aloud. “And they didn’t jump. So how?”

The guilt emanating from her was a palpable force, thick as the mist outside. It was the guilt of a keeper of secrets who had tried, too late, to offer a truth, and had instead delivered a man to his death. Elias Crowe saw the Gothic weight of it on her, the inheritance of a sin she hadn’t committed but had faithfully, silently, carried.
He stood up, his joints stiff. “Get some rest, Eleanor Vance.”

“Detective,” she called out as he reached the door. He turned. Her helpless eyes found his. “Find the thing in the gears. Please.”
