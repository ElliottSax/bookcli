Chapter 5: The Landlord

The rebellion began, as most doomed things do, with a simple plan. Leo would burn a single page. It wasn’t even a significant page. It was from the later section of Thorne’s diary, a tedious account of a dispute with a greengrocer over the quality of turnips. The handwriting was dense, the ink a rust-brown that looked too much like dried blood.
As he stood in the center of the living room, the page held over a ceramic bowl he’d taken from the kitchen, the air was thick with an expectation he couldn’t quite articulate. The usual soundtrack—the drip from the kitchen tap, the muffled thump of a neighbor’s bass—had faded into a heavy silence. The only sound was his own breathing, a slow, measured inhale and exhale that was sapping his will.
He could smell the paper now, dry and slightly sweet like old books. The bowl should have contained a solution of dish soap and water, a dissipating threat of wreckage, but instead, it sat empty, a hollow promise of cancellation. A single match, cold in the darkness, remained on the counter, an afterimage of preparation.
Just a page. It’s nothing.
But what were pages if not fragments of a self, torn away from the writer? The inaccessibility of Thorne’s thoughts, the intrusion of his will, and the ontological doubts that followed made Leo increasingly uncertain about what his own thoughts looked like when written out. The faint tremble in his fingers, where the paper oddly pliant, like skin left too long in bathwater, spoke volumes about the tenebrous quality of Thorne’s residuals.
He flicked the lighter. The flame sprang up, a jaunty, ordinary yellow that briefly illuminated the darkness. He brought it to the corner of the page. The lick of flame sounded dry, airless, and he disjointed shiver flow over his skin.
The coldness wasn’t sudden, but its touch confirmed his suspicions: the hesitation, the dead-weight wrapping skyward his nerves as a living being began crumbling, literally decaying. The flame kissed the page. A brown curl, a wisp of smoke.
And then the fear hit.
It did not come from him. It flooded in, bypassing his mind, seizing his nervous system directly. It was a pure, animal terror of dissolution, a certainty that the universe itself was unraveling from the edges inward, and he was at the epicenter of that un-becoming. It was the horror of the void, not as a concept, but as a physical destination.
His lungs locked. The lighter fell from nerveless fingers, extinguishing on the carpet. The page fluttered after it, its charred corner a tiny, black eye.
Leo was on his knees, then curled on his side, fetal. He could not draw breath. His heart was a frantic bird battering its ribs. The room did not spin; it pulled away, elongating, the walls stretching into a dark tunnel at the end of which was absolute nothingness. He was going to cease. Not die. Cease. Be unmade. Be forgotten.
No not nothing never nothing must remain must persist I MUST—

The voice was a gale in his head, but the terror was in his cells. He clawed at the carpet, trying to anchor himself to reality, but the fibers insubstantial as ash. This was Thorne’s fear. Not of death, but of oblivion. The core terror of a man who built his immortality from other men’s clay. And it was now Leo’s autonomic response.
The attack subsided by inches, leaving him shivering in a pool of cold sweat. It took twenty minutes for his vision to clear, for the sensation of infinite cold to retreat from his fingertips. He lay there, smelling his own sour breath and the faint, acrid scent of the singed paper.
The rebellion was over. He had not been fighting an invader; he had been fighting a law of physics. Thorne was not a guest. He was the landlord. And Leo had simply tried to burn a piece of the property.
The physical changes that followed were both a blurring and a sharpening. He stopped crossing streets. Not out of fear of traffic, but of something far worse: confrontation.
