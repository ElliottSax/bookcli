The reflection was waiting.
It was her, of course. But it was the her of the last few days: back straight, skin glowing, hair falling in a perfect, artful cascade. It wore her face, but the expression was one of serene, infinite patience. It didn’t move. It just watched.
Maya’s heart hammered against her ribs. She took a step forward, then another, her arm trembling. The air grew thicker, resistant, as if she were wading through syrup. A low hum, just at the edge of hearing, vibrated in her molars.
“You’re not me,” she whispered, her voice cracking.
The reflection’s lips did not move, but the thought bloomed in Maya’s mind, cool and smooth as a river stone: I am the you that matters. The you that will be seen.
“Stop it!” she screamed, and lunged the final distance.
She threw the drop cloth over the ornate frame. For a single, glorious second, it was covered. The oppressive weight in the room lightened. She saw the lumpy shape of the cloth, the ghost of the mirror beneath, and a ragged gasp of relief escaped her. It was that simple. Simply don’t look.
Then, the hum escalated into a shriek of tearing fabric.
Not a sound in the air, but a shriek inside the matter itself. The drop cloth didn’t slide or fall. It convulsed, the center of it bulging outward as if an invisible fist were punching through from the other side. Then, with a series of sharp, precise rips, the canvas tore itself apart. Long, deliberate shreds, as if slashed by a manicurist’s razor. The mirror was clear again.
Her reflection stood exactly as before, untouched. Not a hair out of place. But now, the corner of its mouth held the faintest, most chilling suggestion of a smile. It was the smile of a parent watching a toddler throw a tantrum.
Maya stumbled back, a sob catching in her throat. The intellectual understanding from Silas was now a visceral, bodily truth. This was not a passive object. It was a occupant.

The sleek buildings revealed themselves as grimy, pockmarked monoliths, their windows like dead, cataract-filled eyes. The people on the sidewalk… their postures were pathetic—slumped, shuffling, awkward. Their clothes were garish, clashing fabrics hanging off ill-considered frames. She could smell them through the glass: a nauseating cocktail of exhaust, cheap perfume, unwashed hair, and the sour tang of anxiety-sweat.
A beautiful, crippling anxiety, more potent than any she’d ever known, washed over her. It wasn’t fear of danger, but of profound, existential ugliness. The outside world was a poorly designed, malfunctioning organism. It was loud, abrasive, stupid. To step into it would be to immerse herself in a septic tank. Her skin crawled at the very thought. The door at her back was no longer a barrier keeping her in; it was a shield, protecting her from the horrific, disordered reality beyond.
A whimper escaped her. She dug her perfect nails into her scalp, trying to claw the perception out. But it was fixed in place, a new, terrible lens through which to see everything. The entity had weaponized her own aesthetic sensitivity, her lifelong craving for order and beauty, and turned it against the entire world. The ultimate isolation. Nowhere was safe. Only in the reflection.
Tears of pure despair tracked through her perfect makeup. She had no fight left. The will to rebel had been scoured out by this exquisite, personalized horror. She was trapped in the one place in the universe that didn’t revolt her: the apartment curated by her own parasitic reflection.
She didn’t remember getting up. She was simply drawn, a scrap of metal to a magnet, back to the bedroom doorway.
The mirror waited. Her reflection was no longer static. It had moved. It now stood in the center of the glass, its hands clasped gently in front of it. Its face was a picture of compassionate concern.
“What do you want?” Maya rasped, her voice raw.
The reflection’s lips moved. This time, the sound came from the glass itself, a voice like polished glass and distant wind chimes. It was her voice, but filtered through something alien, every rough edge smoothed away.
“We want what you have always wanted, Maya. We are simply more… effective.”

“Who is ‘we’?”

“A simplification. I am the process. The sculptor to your marble. You may call me Eidolon.”

The name hung in the air, cold and classical. A phantom. An ideal image.
“Eidolon,” Maya repeated, the word tasting of ash.
“You struggle against the chisel,” Eidolon said, tilting its—her—head. “You mistake pain for imperfection. The transition is… jarring. I understand. Your previous self clings like a stubborn stain.”

“My previous self is me!”

“Was,” Eidolon corrected gently. “A flawed draft. Full of good intentions but poor execution. You desired success. Admiration. A life of impact and beauty. You begged the universe for it. I am the answer.”

The logic was watertight, insane, and horribly persuasive. It reframed her terror as a failure of vision.

“You will be,” Eidolon promised, its voice a soothing balm. “The most radiant, potent version of ‘you’ that is possible. The you that is never overlooked. The you whose very presence commands a room. The you whose work changes paradigms. Is that not what you lay awake at night craving?”

It was. Every word. It had been the silent soundtrack of her adult life.
