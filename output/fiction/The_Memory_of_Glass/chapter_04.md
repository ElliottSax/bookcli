Chapter 4: A Room That Remembers

The silence in The White Room was not an absence. It was a held breath. Evelyn stood just inside the sealed door, her cane extended but not tapping, her head tilted as if listening to the hum of a dormant machine. The air was cooler here, still tainted with the faint, astringent ghost of forensic cleaners, layered over the older, deeper scent of dust, old varnish, and the peculiar, ozone-like smell of the glass cases. It was the smell of stopped time.
“You have two hours, Ms. Vance.” Alistair Thorne’s voice came from the doorway, tight with bureaucratic anxiety. “The board was very clear. And Mr. Davies here is to be with you at all times.”

“Understood, Mr. Thorne. And thank you.” Evelyn kept her voice soft, non-confrontational. She had used every ounce of her remaining professional capital—invoking her father’s legacy, her own unimpeachable record before this mess, and a fabricated, meek desire for “closure”—to get this. She presence of the sound engineer, Ben Davies, a young man whose hand she’d shaken in Thorne’s office. His grip had been calloused, his greeting quiet. He smelled of solder and coffee.
“Where would you like to start?” Ben asked. His voice was closer now. He moved with a carefulness she recognized; people often moved carefully around her, as if she were made of the same glass as the exhibits.
“The room needs to speak first,” Evelyn said. “Before we ask it questions, we need to hear its voice. Its structure.”

She walk, her cane whispering against the polished marble floor. Her mental map, constructed over years of work and sharpened to a painful clarity on the night of the murder, superimposed itself over the physical space. Case A: Venetian goblets, twelve feet from the door. Case B: pre-Roman fragments, against the north wall. The central plinth, empty now, where the disputed Hellenistic vase had been. She moved toward the north-east corner, the place from which, according to her ears, the first cry had originated.
“This is the center of it,” she said, stopping. “I need you to do a sweep of the room’s natural acoustics. Impulse responses, reverberation times. But most of all, I need you to map anomalies. Dead zones. Places where sound doesn’t behave as it should.”

Ben got to work. She heard the unzipping of his equipment case, the gentle clack of switches, the low hum of a speaker being placed on the floor. “I’ll use a starter pistol,” he replied. “It’s a clean, sharp impulse. We’ll record it from multiple mics.”

The first shot, when it came, was not a gunshot but a precise, percussive crack that appeared to tear the held-breath silence of the room. Evelyn flinched, not at the sound, but at the way it moved. This time, she focused entirely on the auditory aftermath. The sound wave spread, hit surfaces, and collapsed. But not uniformly. To her left, toward the center of the room, the echo died almost instantly, as if swallowed by a pillow. Yet a high, ringing tail of the sound seemed appeared to near the ceiling above the doorway.
“There’s a dead zone about eight feet west of the central plinth,” Ben confirmed, his voice tinged with scientific fascination. “And a strong, focused reflection coming from the arch above the entrance. It’s not random. It’s deliberate.”

A cold clarity washed over Evelyn. “The architect, Moreau. He was obsessed with synesthesia—making visual art evoke sound, texture, even taste. He didn’t simply build a gallery. He built an instrument.”

For the next hour, they mapped the room’s sonic landscape. Ben placed speakers in different locations—where she’d heard the assailant, where she’d heard the victim fall—and recorded the results from her listening post by the north wall. The data revealed a world of illusion.
A sound generated from the south-west corner, near the door, arrived at her ear seeming to come from near the east wall. Her mind went back to the forensic report, the contradiction that had bothered her. The blood spatter, glass fragments, and physics of the fall all pointed to the southeast corner. "Antique eyeglasses," she breathed. "Pre-20th century. Hand-ground crown glass. That's your mysterious silica. Not from a museum case - from someone's face."

Evelyn lifted her cane, her heart hammering a new, frantic rhythm against her ribs. “Yes,” she said, her voice miraculously steady. “I’m coming.”

She stepped onto the elevator, the image of defeated compliance. But in her mind, she was still in the hallway, her cane on that tile. The click echoed, not in the claustrophobic air, but in the dark, widening chambers of the mystery. They could lock her out of The White Room. They could try to silence her. But the museum itself, it seemed, had just whispered a secret through the sole of her shoe. The puzzle was not in a room that remembered.
