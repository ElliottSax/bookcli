Chapter 6: Echoes in the Dark

The border was not a line, but a breath held too long and then released into silence. One moment, the world was a study in muted greys and dying ochres, the light thin and sour but present. The next, Elara stepped through an unseen membrane, and all external light was extinguished. It did not fade; it was swallowed. The effect was so absolute it less like entering darkness and more like being plunged into the depths of a cold, starless sea.
Her own lantern, the one Alistair had insisted she take—its casing etched with runes of containment—became not just a tool, but a sovereign territory. Its glow, which in the outer wastes had been a humble companion, now asserted itself as a tiny, defiant kingdom. It pushed back the void for a radius of perhaps ten feet, painting the ground beneath her boots in shaky strokes of amber. Beyond that, the blackness was not an absence, but a substance. It pressed against the light’s edge, patient and viscous.
The air changed. It lost the mineral tang of the wastes and grew still and close, carrying a scent she could not place—old paper, damp wool, and beneath it, the faint, sweet rot of forgotten flowers. Sound died here, too. Her footsteps, which should have crunched on the barren ground, made no noise. Her own breathing was loud in her ears, a ragged tide. She was a bubble of sensation adrift in a perfect sensory void.
She walked for what like hours, though time had shed its skin. The compass was useless, its needle spinning in lazy, drunken circles. She navigated by the pounding of her heart, by the simple, terrible need to move forward. The lantern’s light bobbed, a luminous prow cutting through the dark sea.
As she moved, the temperature in the air shifted. A chill crept in, seeping into her bones and setting her teeth to chatter. The smell of old paper grew stronger, and with it, the memory of late nights spent in the library, surrounded by dusty tomes and flickering candles. Elara's fingers eked out toward the lantern, as if seeking the comfort of its warm light.
The silence that shrouded her was oppressive, heavy with unspoken emotions. It was as if the shadows themselves had a quiet whispering of doubts, fears, and half-remembered dreams that echoed through every waking moment. The light of the lantern sparked against this, a tribal drum beating a savage counter-cadence to the hesitant silence that hung around her.
And then, the dark give back.
It started with a smell. A sudden, piercing ribbon of lilac and vanilla—her mother’s perfume. The scent was so vivid, so violently specific, that Elara gasped and stopped, her hand flying to her chest as if wounded. It was there for three heartbeats, a ghost in her nostrils, and then it was gone, leaving behind only the old-paper smell and a longing so acute it like a dislocation.
She took another step, and the light from her lantern fell upon something that should not have been. A porch swing, its wood painted a flaking white, one chain slightly rusted. It hung from nothing, suspended in the void. As the light touched it, ito move with a soft, familiar creak—erk-tch, erk-tch—the sound of a thousand summer evenings. She could see the indentations on the seat cushion. She could almost feel the ghost of the warm wood beneath her childhood thighs. She reached a trembling hand out, and her fingers passed through the armrest. The vision shivered, like a reflection in a pond struck by a stone, and dissolved into swirling motes of shadow that bled back into the greater dark.
“An echo,” she whispered, the first words she had spoken since entering. Her voice was small, consumed by the silence.
The Hinterlands were not a geography. Alistair’s maps, his careful calculations, they were the logic of a world that did not apply here. This was a psychology. A gallery of loss. She understood it now, not with her mind, but with the marrow of her bones. This was where shadows went when they were severed, when their owners died or forgot them. And shadows were not just silhouettes; they were impressions, the negatives of moments, the emotional residue left on the fabric of the world. They pooled here, in this state of being, this repository for all that had slipped away.
The understanding was a cold weight in her stomach. Her own shadow, fraying at her heels, twitched in sympathy.
She moved on, the phantom sensations coming more frequently. The cool smoothness of a specific river stone. The taste of her father’s bitter black tea, stolen from his cup. The texture of her nursery wallpaper, raised velvet roses under her small fingertips. Each memory was a flare of exquisite pain, illuminating a corridor of her past she had worked for decades to wall off. They were not welcome visits. They were ambushes.
"Echoes," she muttered to herself, the word feeling like a fragile defense against the army of recollections that sought to overwhelm her. She squinted through the lamp's light, half-expecting to see them—those things that were no longer things, not anymore. Her breath was shallow, lumped up like the kindling in her chest. She knew that if she stopped walking, if she stopped moving, she would dissolve into the encounter like a face erased by acid.
She wanted to recall their names—Heidi, Liu, Xiao—and her stories, words than they couldn't say to their creators as they slipped away into those shrouds.
