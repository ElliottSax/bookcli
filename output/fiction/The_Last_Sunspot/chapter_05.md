Chapter 5: Cracks in the Light

The silence that followed the failed weave was not the quiet of peace, but the hollow, ringing aftermath of a shattering, a vibration that appeared to linger in the teeth and bones. For two days, they travelled through a forest that less like a living thing and more like a preserved corpse. Elara did not hum. She did not comment on the stubborn curl of a fern or the way the light, what little of it filtered through the perpetual grey canopy, dusted the moss in specks of tarnished silver. She simply walked, a pace behind Kaelen, her light a muted, guttering thing, its usual honeyed scent faded to the faint, sweet-rot smell of dying lilies.
It was a relief, Kaelen told himself.
He found he could think without the constant, grating pressure of her radiance against his senses, that particular brightness that always like a thumb pressed against his closed eyelids. The headache that had been his constant companion since she’d blundered into his territory receded to a dull, familiar throb at the base of his skull. He could hear the forest again—the wet, peeling sigh of damp bark, the distant, metallic plink… plink… of water seeping through mineral veins, the papery skittering of carapaced things that preferred the grey. The air tasted of damp soil and cold stone, clean in its emptiness. He could almost pretend he was alone again, just a shadow among shadows.
Almost.
Her presence was now a different weight. Not a sunburn, but a chill, a pocket of stillness that sucked the warmth from the air around her. He’d glance back, expecting the defiant tilt of her chin, the ready, infuriating smile that seemed fueled by some internal, inexhaustible furnace. Instead, he saw a profile of chalk, her eyes fixed on the treacherous path but seeing nothing, her gaze turned inward to some bleak landscape. The golden filaments in her hair were tarnished, dull as old brass, and her skin, usually warmed with a glow from within, was pale as moonstone, almost translucent at the temples. She looked… breakable. It unsettled him more than her brightness had. Anger was a territory he knew; this quiet dissolution was not.
On the afternoon of the second day, the path narrowed into a ravine of slick, black rock streaked with mineral deposits that gleamed like frozen tears. A thin stream, silent and dark as polished obsidian, traced its bottom, cutting through beds of rounded, water-worn stone. The air grew colder, carrying the smell of wet stone, of iron, and something else—a faint, coppery tang, like old blood on the wind.
“We should cross,” Kaelen said, his voice rough from disuse, the words scraping out of him. It was the first object either had spoken since the observatory.
Elara merely nodded, her movements mechanical, as if her body were a puppet on slack strings. She stepped onto the first moss-slick stone, a flat, dark slate beaded with moisture. Her foot, clad in a boot now scarred and mud-caked, slipped without purchase. There was no gasp, no flailing of arms. She just fall, silently, toward the ink-dark water that would swallow her light whole.
Kaelen’s shadow, longer and more substantial in the gorge’s deep gloom, darted out from his feet like a living thing, a swift stain spreading across the rock. It wrapped around her waist, a band of cool, insubstantial darkness that none the less held firm, its touch like dense, static-charged silk. It steadied her, lifting her slightly. For a second, their eyes met over the dark water. In hers, he saw not gratitude, but a kind of vacant surprise, as if she’d forgotten he was there, that anyone was there at all, that a body could be saved from a fall.
He set her down gently on the far bank, the shadow retreating into him with a sensation like a cool drink drawn back into his veins. “Watch your step,” he muttered, the habitual edge to his voice blunted, made hollow.
“Thank you,” she whispered. The words were ash in her mouth, sound without substance.
They made camp an hour later in a shallow cave, little more than a scoop in the ravine wall, its ceiling hung with fibrous, pale roots that dripped a slow, cold sap. Elara sat on a rock worn smooth by ancient water, her knees drawn up to her chest, staring at her hands as if they belonged to a stranger. She tried to summon a spark, simply a little one, to push back the creeping chill. A flicker, wan and sickly yellow, danced on her palm for a heartbeat before guttering out with a faint hiss and the smell of ozone. She closed her fist, the nails biting into her palm, swallowing hard against the lump of failure in her throat.
Kaelen watched from across the small space, his back against stone that leeched his body heat. He’d woven the cave’s entrance into a veil of shifting gloom, a simple camouflage of deepened shadows and suggested rock faces. The act was effortless now that her light wasn’t fighting him, wasn’t unconsciously trying to bleach every darkness it touched. He should have been pleased. This was easier. This was what he’d wanted—her fire banked, her noise stilled.
The silence, however, had become a third presence, thick and demanding.
“Your silence is louder than your chatter,” he found himself saying, the words escaping before he could cage them.
She didn’t look up, but her shoulders tightened almost imperceptibly. “I don’t know what to say.” Her voice was the rustle of dead leaves.
“You always did before. Nonsense about hope and… sunbeams.” He gestured vaguely, his own fingers looking like pale spiders in the dim light.
A tremor went through her, a fine vibration he could see in the line of her neck. “It was never nonsense.” Her voice was so low he had to lean forward to catch it, the sound swallowed by the damp cave. “It was a recipe. A precise measurement. One part courage, two parts cheer, a steady simmer of optimism. The perfect fuel for the light.” She finally lifted her gaze. The vibrant blue he’d known was dim, clouded over like a winter sky, the color of storm clouds heavy with unshed snow. “The cook seems to have lost the instructions.”

He leaned back against the cold stone, feeling its ridges press through his cloak. “So it was an act.” He meant it to sound like an accusation, a confirmation of his long-held suspicion, but it came out flat.
“No.” The word was sharp, a brittle snap. Then it softened, crumbling. “Yes. I don’t know.” She pressed the heels of her hands against her eyes. “It was a duty. From the moment my magic manifested, they told me what I was: a light. The last, best reminder of what the world was. Joy wasn’t a feeling, Kaelen. It was a… a public utility. You turned the tap, and light came out. And if the reservoir runs dry…” She trailed off, looking past him into the small, dark fire he’d conjured from nothing but concentrated shadow and will. It gave no heat, only a faint, purple-tinged light that made the cave walls seem to waver and retreat, and it smelled of cold smoke and midnight.
He understood duty. His was a shroud, a silence, a bearing of the unbearable weight of memory. His was the solemn vow to remember the precise cost of the Cataclysm, to tend the graves of the world with shadows so they would not be forgotten. He’d never had to pretend to be sad. The sorrow was the marrow of his bones, the rhythm of his breath. Her confession—that her light was a performance, a sustained act of will—didn’t feel like a victory. It like discovering the sun was painted on a canvas.
“They never taught you what to do when the light fails,” he said, not a question but a statement of profound, institutional neglect.
A single, clean tear escaped, tracing a glistening path through the fine dust on her cheek. It caught the odd light of his shadow-fire, a tiny, fallen star extinguished on her skin. “They replied it wouldn’t. They said the light was fueled by the heart, and a Sunspot’s heart was pure, an endless wellspring.” She opened her hands, staring at the empty palms. “A broken pump can’t push water, Kaelen. What if my heart is just… broken?” Her voice cracked on the last word, not into a sob, but into a whisper of pure, unadorned fear.
The confession hung in the cold, still air of the cave, fragile and shocking as a crystal bell. He had no answer, no scripture from his own grim traditions to offer. She uncurled slightly, stretching her legs out, and stared at his shadow-fire. “When I slipped,” she began slowly, “your shadow caught me. It was cold. But it was solid.
