A man nearby, his glasses reflecting the case lights, leaned in, his breath fogging the glass for a second before he moved on, unaware of the personal earthquake under his nose. She found Leo across the room. He was listening to an elderly historian, nodding, but his posture was a line of tension. His dark suit drank the shadows until he shifted, and a blade of light from a sconce caught the side of his face. The historian’s voice, the clink of a wine glass, the rustle of a program—it all receded, muffled as if she’d plunged her head underwater. For a heartbeat, it was just them, the silent understanding a thread pulled so taut across the crowded room she feared it might vibrate, might sing, or snap. In that suspended moment, her gaze flickered. She saw Chiara watching Leo. The older woman’s expression was a complex alloy: pride in the set of her shoulders, a profound melancholy in the slight droop of her mouth. Her dove-grey suit was a slab of calm marble amidst the riot of jewel-toned silks and velvets. The fabric was fine but severe, the cut forgiving nothing. Chiara’s hands, clasped before her, were a landscape of raised veins and age spots, the hands of a woman who had gardened, arranged flowers, turned countless pages. Her eyes, a deep, liquid brown, met Elara’s. There was no smile, only a slow, deliberate blink—an eyelid falling like a curtain on a private stage—and a nod so slight it was almost imperceptible. The weight of their shared secret was not a burden they carried together, but a cold stone each held separately in their own pocket. Elara felt its chill through the silk of her dress. A gentle pressure on her elbow. Chiara was beside her, the scent of lavender and faint camphor cutting through the peonies. Her touch was dry, papery. “The time has come,” Chiara said, her voice a low rasp that didn’t carry beyond the two of them. It wasn’t a question. Her eyes were on the podium. “You’re ready.” The words were stripped of encouragement; they were a simple statement of fact, and in their flatness, Elara heard the old woman’s own resignation to what was about to be unearthed. The crowd settled into rows of cane-backed chairs that creaked in protest. A manufactured hush descended, broken by the subcutaneous groan of old floorboards, the distant, watery sigh of the Adige River beyond the thick walls, and the collective rustle of bodies seeking comfort. Elara’s mouth was dry, tasting of candle smoke and faint dread. She took a deep breath, but it didn’t settle her; it just filled her lungs with the heavy, spiced air. She was the master of these words, had arranged them with surgical precision, but her heart was a frantic bird in her ribcage. Her fingers found the edges of the folder in her hand, the crisp cardstock a poor substitute for the vellum inside. She didn’t need to look.
