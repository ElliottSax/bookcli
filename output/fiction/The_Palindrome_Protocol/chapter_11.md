It wasn’t a true silence, of course. The bullpen of the 12th Precinct was a cacophony of ringing phones, shouted congratulations, the clatter of keyboards filing final reports. The mayor had called. The commissioner was coming down for a photo-op. Before him lay the material ghost of the Palindrome Killer: evidence bags containing fragments of polished oak from the symmetrical chamber, a sheaf of Hovenab’s architectural schematics, a single, unused length of copper wire. To anyone else, it was debris. To Thorne, it was a still-vibrating chord, a fossilized scream in a language only his fractured senses could parse.

A vast, completed labyrinth rendered in the cold, translucent blue of antique cathedral glass. He could see all its paths, its dead ends and looping corridors, but it was frozen. Brittle. As if the events at the chamber had not just stopped a bomb, but had halted time itself into a perfect, fragile sculpture. The sensation was one of profound claustrophobia. He was trapped inside a finished story.

“We did,” Thorne said, his own voice sounding distant, filtered through the glass of his internal maze.

The smiles around him were too wide, the relief too giddy. They were celebrating the sealing of a tomb, unaware they were now living inside it.

A chill, colder than the prison’s air conditioning, traced Thorne’s spine. He said nothing.

“Is that your last riddle?” Thorne asked, forcing his voice to remain flat.

“It is the first and the last. Solve that, and you will see the shape of everything.” Hovenab leaned back. “We are done now.”
