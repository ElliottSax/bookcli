Chapter 9: Labyrinth’s Heart

To his synesthesia, it was a visual catastrophe. “The pattern isn’t about people. Not anymore.” Thorne finally looked up, and Cruz took an involuntary step back. “It’s the key.” He stood abruptly, sweeping the maps aside. He began to pace, a caged animal tracing the dimensions of its cell. “The letters. We’ve been reading them in the order they were left. But he doesn’t think linearly. He thinks in circles. In mirrors.”

He gathered the seven evidence bags, ignoring protocol, and laid them out not in a line, but in a rough circle on the floor, with the hub location—the Archive—as their center. He crouched, his body aching, his senses screaming as the tactile feel of the plastic bags collided with the visual noise of the letters inside.

“S… T… A… S… I… O… N,” he recited, clockwise. Then he started again, counter-clockwise from the same point. “S… O… I… S… T… A…”

He went silent. The room seemed to contract, the hum of the computers fading into a distant whine.

“Say it out loud,” he whispered.

Cruz knelt beside him, her knee popping. She pointed, her finger moving around the circle. “S-T-A-S-I-O-N. Station.”

“Now the other way. From the same start point.”

She traced backwards. “S-O-I-S-T-A… Soista? That’s nothing.”

“You’re reading it as a word. He’s playing a game. The hub is the mirror. Read towards the hub from each point.” His voice was tight, electric. “The first site, the ‘S’. The letter faces the hub. It’s the first letter of a word that radiates inward. The second site, the ‘T’. It’s the second letter. Follow the circle.”

Cruz’s eyes widened. She moved her finger from bag to bag, speaking slowly. “S… T… A… S… I… O… N.” She completed the circle. “It just spells ‘STASION’. That’s misspelled.”

“Now,” Thorne said, his breath shallow. “Start from the point directly opposite the first ‘S’. The mirror point. Read inwards from there.”

She found the bag opposite—the ‘O’ from the observatory. She moved inward, her lips forming the letters. “O… I… S… T… A… S… Y.”

She froze.

“Oistasy?” she said.

“Symmetry,” Thorne breathed. The word hung in the air, cold and absolute. “The ‘Y’ is missing. The final letter. The final victim. The pattern isn’t ‘STATION’. It’s ‘SYMMETRY’, mirrored around the hub. S-Y-M-M-E-T-R-Y. Seven letters. We have seven sites. But we only have one ‘M’ in the sequence. He’s using the hub as the mirror to create the second ‘M’. The hub is the fold. The point of reflection.”

Cruz stared at the circle of plastic bags. The clinical evidence of seven murders had just resolved into a single, monstrous word. “The final victim is the ‘Y’.”

“Not a person whose name starts with Y,” Thorne said, rising, a terrible clarity burning through the sensory static. “The ‘Y’ in geometry is the vertical axis. The point of convergence. The place where all lines meet.” He stabbed his finger at the map, at the heart of the labyrinth. “The final victim is the hub itself. Or everyone in it. At the moment of perfect symmetry. Noon. He’s going to blow up the Archive.”

Cruz was already on her radio, barking orders. “All teams, converge on the Municipal Archive! Evacuate the perimeter! Sweep the building! Now!”

Thorne’s light danced over the walls, revealing decades of graffiti, calcified mineral deposits like frozen tears, and the ghostly outlines of painted numbers and arrows from a forgotten era.
