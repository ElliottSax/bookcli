Chapter 6

The rain began as a whisper against the conservatory glass, a soft hiss that made the world outside the old restaurant blur and run like wet paint. Inside, the air was thick with the scent of damp earth from the potted ferns and the lingering, ghostly perfume of last night’s vanilla pod and seared scallops. Leo stood at the long, stainless steel counter, a single, imperfect morel mushroom turning in his fingers. It felt like a tiny, desiccated brain, its honeycombed cap a labyrinth holding the taste of the forest floor. He was supposed to be prepping for the evening’s final service, but his hands were still.

Across the room, Anya was polishing wine glasses. The soft shush of linen on crystal was the only sound for a long time. Since the argument two nights ago—since she’d called him a curator of ghosts and he’d accused her of wanting to burn the place down just to feel the heat—a fragile, exhausting truce had settled. They spoke only of necessary things: The delivery is late. Table six needs a vegan alternative. The extractor fan in the walk-in is making that sound again.

But the unsaid things crowded the room, louder than the rain.

Leo placed the morel down carefully on the cutting board. He looked at his hands, the nicks and scars a pale map of his career. A burn from a spluttering pan of duck fat, a thin white line from a mandoline that had slipped years ago. They were the hands of a cook, not a maestro. Not his father’s hands. Antonio Rossi’s hands had been broad, capable of effortlessly filleting a whole turbot or gently shaping a perfect tortellino, all while holding a cigarette that never seemed to ash into the food. Leo’s hands just worked. They followed instructions, sought consistency. They had built The Last Course into a temple of precision, but now the temple felt empty, and he was just a tired priest.

“He’s not coming, you know.”

Anya’s voice, flat and clear, cut through his thoughts. She didn’t look up from the glass she was holding to the light.

“Who?”

“Don’t,” she said, finally setting the glass down with a quiet click. “The critic. Michelin. Whatever phantom you’ve been waiting for all these years. The rain’s biblical, Leo. The road from the city will be flooded at the bridge. No one is driving an hour for dinner tonight.”

He felt a hot needle of irritation, followed immediately by a cold wave of relief. She was probably right. The reservation book showed only three tables: a regular elderly couple who came every Thursday, a pair of local businessmen who ordered the same steak and Bordeaux, and a four-top that had been a last-minute online booking, likely tourists who didn’t know about the road.

“We serve who comes,” he said, the old mantra sounding hollow.

“We do,” she agreed, but her tone was different. It wasn’t defiance. It was exhaustion. “But maybe tonight, we could just… cook.”

He looked at her. She was wearing a simple black t-shirt under her unbuttoned chef’s jacket, her hair twisted into a messy knot. There were shadows under her eyes. He realized, with a start, that he didn’t know where she went when she left here. He knew she rented a room above a hardware store in the village, but he had never asked about it. He knew her palate was exceptional, that she could identify a missing grain of salt in a litre of bisque, but he didn’t know where she was from, not really. Somewhere up north. A town with a closed-down factory. That was all.

“What does that mean?” he asked, the question genuine.

She walked over to the walk-in, pulled the heavy door open. The cold mist curled out like a breath. “It means we have three tables. It means the produce delivery didn’t come because of the weather.
