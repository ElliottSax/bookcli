Chapter 4: The Anatomy of a Miracle

The Tower of the Penitent Spire was not a hospital. It was a vertical tomb, smelling of cold stone, old rust, and the faint, sweet decay of mortar-lichen. The air inside was heavy with damp earth and the musty tang of old books. But to Alistair Finch, who had been given nothing and was expected to do everything, its three circular chambers were a kingdom. His kingdom. A grim, clinical, and morally fraught kingdom.
He stood at the slit window of the highest chamber, his new infirmary, and looked out over Elyria. The city was a carcass picked at by time. Roofs sagged like tired diaphragms, their wooden beams creaking as the wind howled through the streets. The cobblestones, once polished smooth, now wore a dull, cracked patina, like the skin of a long-dead animal. In the distance, the ever-present haze of the Withering blurred the horizon, a smudge of jaundiced yellow against a leaden sky. Up here, the air was clear, but it carried the chill of altitude and isolation. Alistair shivered, despite the thick woolen cloak wrapped around his shoulders.
His hands, scrubbed raw, rested on the rough-hewn sill. They were his only familiar tools in this place. Down below, in the second chamber, his 'apprentices' clattered pewter bowls. There were two gangly youths from the citadel's servant quarters, their faces smudged with soot and their eyes wide with wonder, and a grim-faced woman with red-rimmed eyes who had lost a husband to a festering wound. The clatter was a staccato beat, like the ticking of a clock, as they worked to sterilize the equipment. Alistair lectured them not on humors of blood and bile, but on contamination, sepsis, and cross-contact. They listened with a mix of awe and terror, as if he were revealing forbidden secrets. To them, he supposed, he was.
He was no longer Dr. Finch, the disgraced surgeon. He was Alistair, the Saint-in-Residence. The Healer from Beyond the Gate. The title was an ill-fitting garment, stitched with hope and desperation, and it chafed him raw. He like a puppet, with the Council pulling the strings.
The miracle for the child, Elara, had been a grotesque pantomime. Under the guttering light of the Council chamber, with her parents' sobs as a backdrop, he had laid hands on her fevered body. He nothing at first, just the dreadful fragility of a system shutting down. Then, a pressure built behind his eyes—a sharp, prismatic pain. The world bleached of color, then flooded with a new layer of perception. He saw the child not as flesh, but as a dim, guttering lamp. The Withering was a viscous shadow, clinging to her core, snuffing the light. And beside him, the volunteer—a farmer with a persistent cough and sunken eyes, who had risked his own life—blazed like a humble candle.
The transfer was not surgery. It was visceral theft. He willed the light from the man into the child. It wasn't a flow, but a tearing, a re-routing of vital pathways he could sense but not comprehend. The farmer gasped, aging a decade in a moment, as the child's cheeks flushed with stolen vitality. The Council called it a miracle. Alistair vomited into a basin afterwards, his own body trembling with a cold, alien exhaustion. He had like he was drowning in the child's fever, like the Withering was a living thing, wrapping around his heart.
Now, in his tower, he experimented. The magic here, they called it the Glimmer. The Vital Essence. It was the fundamental currency of life, and Alistair was its chief accountant. He spent his days poring over ancient texts, searching for clues. He pored over dusty tomes, his eyes scanning the yellowed pages. He spent hours in the laboratory, mixing potions and testing theories, his hands moving with a precision that belied the turmoil in his mind.
As he worked, the silence was oppressive, broken only by the soft clinking of equipment and the occasional creak of the old stone. Alistair like a ghost haunting the tower, a specter of a man who had lost his way. He was trapped in a world of his own making, a world of miracles and monsters, where the lines between reality and fantasy blurred.
The door to the second chamber creaked open, and the apprentices tumbled in, their faces flushed with excitement. "Alistair, sir!" one of them exclaimed, "we've finished the sterilization! We're ready to start on the next patient!"

Alistair turned, his eyes narrowing as he surveyed the chaos. The room was a mess, with equipment scattered everywhere and the air thick with the smell of disinfectant. He surge of frustration, but it was quickly replaced by feelings of calm. He took a deep breath, his eyes locking onto the apprentices.

The apprentices exchanged nervous glances, looking down at the floor. Alistair watched them, searching for any sign of doubt or fear. But they stood tall, their faces set in determined lines.
