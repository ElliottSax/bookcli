The Daily Currents Exercise: Tracking the Weather Within

We begin with a simple, week-long practice I call the Daily Currents Exercise. You will need a small notebook, or a note on your phone. The task is to record three things, three times a day (morning, afternoon, evening), without a single word about productivity. Don’t worry about grammar, spelling, or coherence. Just write down what you feel.

Energy: On a scale of 1-10, what is your charge? Is your body heavy, like a stone warmed by the sun? Or is it buzzing, a live wire? Is it a low, steady hum? You might feel it in your toes, your fingertips, or the pit of your stomach. Write down the sensation, the texture, the temperature. Don’t try to analyze it; just observe it.

For example, on a typical morning, Sarah, a graphic designer and client, might write: "6am: Energy 7, Focus sharp (eyes open, ready to tackle the day), Stress: gentle hum in the background." She might not even realize it, but the gentle hum of the refrigerator, the soft chirping of the birds outside, and the quiet hum of the computer all blend together to create a soothing background noise that helps her focus. The words flow from her pen like a gentle stream, each one a small, precise observation of the world around her.

Focus: Where is your attention? Is it a laser point, a diffuse glow, or a frantic butterfly? Don’t judge its target—just note its quality. Are you focused on a specific task, or are you drifting between thoughts? Are you lost in a daydream, or are you completely absorbed in the present moment? Write down the quality of your focus, not the content.

For instance, on a busy afternoon, Sarah might write: "2pm: Focus diffuse (jumping from screen to window to clock), Energy 4, Stress: tight band around forehead." She might not even realize it, but the constant switching between tasks, the distractions, and the pressure to meet deadlines all contribute to a diffuse focus that's both scattered and exhausting. The words spill out of her pen in a frantic, disjointed rhythm, each one a small, desperate attempt to capture the chaos of her mind.

Stress/Calm: What’s the texture of your inner atmosphere? Is it a still pond, a choppy lake, a storm-tossed sea? Are you feeling calm and centered, or are you on edge, like a coiled spring? Write down the sensation, the feeling, the emotional tone. Don’t try to label it as “good” or “bad”; just observe it.

For example, on a particularly stressful day, Sarah might write: "8pm: Stress: storm-tossed sea (feeling overwhelmed, anxious), Energy 2, Focus: foggy (struggling to concentrate)." The words flow from her pen like a dark, turbulent river, each one a small, desperate attempt to capture the turmoil of her emotions. The sentence is short and simple, but the emotions behind it are complex and multifaceted, like the intricate patterns of a stormy sea.

That’s it. Do not write: “Wasted morning scrolling.” Do write: “10am: Energy 3, Focus diffuse (jumping from screen to window to clock), Stress: choppy lake with undercurrent.” This is meteorology for the soul. You are tracking internal weather patterns, and like any weather, they are not you; they are conditions you are experiencing. The words flow from your pen like a gentle stream, each one a small, precise observation of the world around you.
