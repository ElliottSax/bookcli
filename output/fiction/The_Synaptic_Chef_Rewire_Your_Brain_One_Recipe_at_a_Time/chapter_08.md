
The air in my kitchen was thick with the smell of burnt food. It wasn't the rich smell of a perfectly aged cheese, but the bitter smell of a pancake left unattended. On the counter sat a cast-iron skillet, and inside it, a pancake that had gone from golden-brown to charcoal-black. I'd been distracted by a text, and in moments, the batter had turned into a smoking ruin.
My first instinct was to beat myself up over it. I thought, "You're an idiot. You can't even make a simple pancake. You had one job." This thought pattern was all too familiar, and it wasn't just about the pancake. It was about all the other things I'd messed up recently, like missing a meditation session, skipping the gym, and forgetting about the vegetables in the crisper. One mistake made me feel like I was fundamentally flawed. This is the kind of self-criticism we're going to learn to overcome.
The truth is, one mistake doesn't define your cooking skills. In fact, the best chefs learn more from their mistakes than their successes. When you're cooking, every recipe has its own set of challenges, and you have to make adjustments on the fly. The stove, oven, season, and weather all play a role in the final product. No chef expects to get it perfect every time; they know that their best dishes come from learning from their failures.
When you slip up, your brain's alarm system kicks in. The amygdala lights up, and a critical voice starts telling you that you're not good enough. This voice can come from your past, from a parent, teacher, or earlier version of yourself. It writes a script of self-doubt, which is not only unpleasant but also harmful. It floods your system with stress hormones like cortisol and norepinephrine, which shut down the part of your brain that helps you problem-solve and try again. This self-criticism can undermine the progress you've made and make it harder to get back on track.
As I stood in my kitchen, surrounded by the smell of burnt food, I thought about all the times I'd messed up in the past. I remembered the arguments with friends, the mistakes I'd made in sports, and the times I'd fallen short of my goals. Each failure had made me feel like I wasn't good enough, like I was somehow broken. But as I looked at the burnt pancake, something shifted. I feelings of acceptance wash over me, and my panic fade.
So, I want to teach you a new way to deal with mistakes. I call it the Kitchen Sink Protocol, and it's simple: Pause, Analyze, Adjust, Restart.
Step 1: Pause (And Put Down the Whisk)

When you make a mistake, the first thing to do is stop. Don't try to fix it right away. Don't promise yourself that you'll do better tomorrow. Just pause. When I paused, I noticed the sounds around me, like the hum of the game notifications on my phone, and how they faded into the background as I focused on my breath.
It was almost eerily peaceful. Which ingredient got off track?
