Chapter 4: Roots in the Blood

The soil of Hollow Oak was in Elara's blood. She knew it now, not as a metaphor, but as a grim, physiological fact. The morning after discovering the first lesion—a rough, bark-like patch on her left calf—she found another. This one was a small, weeping circle on her right palm, the skin around it stained a faint, loamy brown. It smelled not of infection, but of damp earth and rotting leaves. When she pressed it, a single grain of black sand, too perfectly round to be natural, emerged. The pain was not sharp; it was a deep, throbbing ache, as if the wound went all the way to the bone and was packed with cold mud.

The blight in the town park had spread. The circle of dead grass was now ten feet across, and the oak at its center wept a viscous, amber sap from a crack in its trunk. Elara stood before it, her own palm throbbing in sympathy. She held her hand up, comparing the patterns in her skin to the broken capillaries around the lesion on her leg. The veins on her hands seemed to pulse in time with the tortured tree. A connection, direct and horrifying, was being sewn between her flesh and the failing land.

The Hollow Oak Historical Society was a tomb of paper and dust, a one-room annex attached to the town’s skeletal library. Elara had taken to arriving before dawn, her movements furtive, driven by a need she couldn't name. The polite fiction of her job—curating exhibits on quilting bees and the founding of the grange—had been abandoned. Now, she hunted.

The records were a chaotic mess, proof of generations of haphazard filing. She found property ledgers, birth and death certificates scrawled in spidery ink, minutes from town council meetings debating the price of cider. And then, tucked behind a moldering ledger labeled "Seed Purchases, 1887-1891," she found the first fragment.

It was a single sheet, brittle as a dried leaf. The heading read, simply, "Account of The Giving." The script was tight, economical.

"October 27th, 1891. The yield is ash in the mouth. The corn is stunted, the apples bitter with rot. The Committee has met. The Offering has been selected. It is agreed: the Soil-Soaked Sacrifice must be made before the last leaf falls. The Heart-Oak hungers. We must feed the roots, or be consumed by them."

Elara's breath fogged in the cold air of the room. Soil-Soaked Sacrifice. Heart-Oak. The words resonated in the hollow of her chest. She thought of the massive, gnarled oak in the center of The Town Square, dominated by the ancient, gnarled 'Heart-Oak', the one from which Hollow Oak took its name. Its roots were said to run under every major street.

Her search became frantic, her fingers leaving smudges of her own earthy discharge on the pages. She found oblique references in financial records: "Payment to J. Harlow for silence, re: the Harvest." "Timber for the pyre, Last Harvest." The word "Harvest" was never capitalized in the old documents except in this specific, terrifying context.

The migraines returned, not as sudden blows, but as a constant, low-pressure presence behind her eyes, smelling of ozone and turned earth. The soil under her fingernails was perpetual now, no matter how fiercely she scrubbed. It was as if her body was slowly transmuting into the stuff of the blighted town.

A week after finding the first fragment, the need for air, for proof that the world outside the records room still functioned, drove her out. She walked, her feet carrying her without conscious direction away from the quaint main street, past the last white picket fences, and into the rolling farmland that cradled the town.

Here, the decay was not picturesque. It was economic, desperate. She saw fields where the corn stood grey and shriveled, like old men's fingers clutching at a dead sky. Orchards where the branches were skeletal, fruit mummified on the bough. The air here didn't smell of rich loam, but of vinegar and defeat.

She was standing at a rusted gate, staring at a field of withered soybean plants, when a voice spoke behind her.

"Ain't a pretty sight, is it?"

She turned, startled. A man in his late forties stood there, his face leathered by sun and wind, his eyes a faded blue. He wore worn coveralls and an expression of profound weariness. He wasn't handsome, but there was a solidity to him, like a fence post that had weathered a hundred storms.

"No," Elara said, her voice raspy from disuse. "It's not."

"Liam Cauley," he said, nodding toward the field. "This is what's left of mine."

"Elara Vance. I'm… at the historical society."

A faint, humorless smile touched his lips. "Diggin' up our glorious past? Might find more life in those old books than out here."

"What happened?" she asked, gesturing to the field.

"What always happens," Liam sighed, unlatching the gate. "Come on, if you want a closer look. Just don't breathe too deep."

She followed him into the field. Up close, the blight was more sinister. The plants weren't just dead; they were wrong. The stems were twisted into spirals, the leaves curled into tight, blackened fists. She crouched, against her better judgment, and touched the soil. It was cold and granular, like ash. And there, amidst the grey, were tiny, pearl-like nodules. They looked exactly like the thing that had come out of her palm.

"It's in the ground," Liam said, watching her. "Sucks the life right out. Tried everything. Different rotations, new seed, more fertilizer, less fertilizer. It's like the land itself just… quit. Or got poisoned." He kicked a clump of dead soybeans. "My granddad said his granddad talked about a bad time like this. Called it 'the land going fallow in its soul.' Superstitious nonsense, I thought."

Elara straightened, wiping her hand on her trousers. "What did they do about it? Back then?"

Liam looked at her, his gaze sharpening. "Why? You find something in those books of yours?"

She hesitated, the words 'Soil-Soaked Sacrifice' clotting in her throat. "Just curious. Patterns. History repeating."

Liam studied her for a long moment, then shrugged. "Stories. About sacrifices made to save us?
