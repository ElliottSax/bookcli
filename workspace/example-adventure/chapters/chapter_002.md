# Chapter 2

Pain woke him.

Not the dull ache of a bruise or the sharp sting of a cut. This was different—a burning sensation that radiated from his chest outward, like his ribcage had been branded from the inside.

Marcus gasped and his eyes snapped open.

Stone ceiling. Carved with more of those symbols, glowing faint silver in the dim light. The air smelled like incense and something metallic, copper maybe, or blood.

He tried to sit up. His body refused. Every muscle felt weighted down, encased in invisible concrete.

A face appeared above him—human this time, not faceless void. A girl, maybe seventeen or eighteen, with dark skin and hair twisted into dozens of small braids that clicked with beads when she moved. Her eyes were gold. Not brown with flecks of gold, but solid metallic gold from edge to edge, no white visible.

"Don't move." Her voice was crisp, accented in a way Marcus couldn't place. "The binding marks need another hour to settle."

"What—" His throat was sandpaper. "What did you do to me?"

"I didn't do anything." She moved out of his line of sight. Water splashed into a cup. "The Weavers performed the Binding. I'm keeping you alive through the aftermath."

She returned with a clay cup and lifted his head with surprising gentleness. The water was cool and tasted faintly of mint. Marcus drank greedily.

"There." She set the cup aside and pressed two fingers to his wrist, checking his pulse. "Your heart rate is stabilizing. Good."

Marcus managed to turn his head. They were in a small chamber carved from dark stone. No windows. A single doorway covered by a curtain of beaded strings. The only light came from those glowing symbols on the ceiling and a brazier in the corner that burned without smoke.

"Where am I?"

"The Sanctum. Deep beneath the forest." The girl pulled a stool next to his stone slab—not a bed, he realized, but an altar of some kind. "You're in the heart of the Weaver's domain."

"The Weavers." Marcus forced the word out. "Those things with no faces."

"They have faces. You can't see them." She examined his eyes, leaning close. Her breath smelled like honey. "Pupils responsive. No signs of rejection. You're adapting better than I expected."

"Adapting to what?" Marcus tried again to move his arm. His fingers twitched. Progress. "What the hell is a Binding?"

The girl sat back and folded her arms. Those gold eyes studied him with an intensity that made his skin prickle.

"You don't know anything, do you? They pulled you through completely blind."

"I touched a box in an abandoned house. Next thing I know, I'm here and those—Weavers—are calling me 'the key' and talking about binding something." The burning in his chest flared. Marcus hissed through his teeth. "What did they bind me to?"

"Not to. Through." She stood and walked to the brazier, adding something from a leather pouch. The flames shifted from orange to pale blue. "The Binding doesn't attach you to anything. It opens you up. Creates channels through your body for magic to flow."

Magic. The word should have sounded ridiculous. But after everything he'd seen, it landed with the weight of truth.

"Why me?"

"Because you're a key." She returned to the stool. "Your world and mine—they're separated by a barrier. The Veil. Nothing can cross it naturally anymore. But certain bloodlines carry resonance. Echo signatures that match on both sides."

"I don't understand."

"Your ancestors came from here." She gestured around them. "Generations ago, when the Veil was thinner. They crossed to your world, had children, built lives. But their magic never left them. It went dormant. Sleeping."

Marcus's head spun. Magic. Other worlds. Ancestors.

"My dad—"

"Probably had the resonance too." The girl's expression softened . "Is he the one who taught you about the house?"

"He's dead." The words came out flat. Marcus had said them so many times they'd lost all meaning. "Car accident. Last year."

Something flickered across her face. Pity? Recognition? It vanished before he could identify it.

"I'm sorry."

Marcus looked away. The symbols on the ceiling pulsed in rhythm with his heartbeat. Or maybe his heartbeat was matching them. Hard to tell.

"What's your name?" he asked.

"Kira." She paused. "Kira Shen. I'm a Mender. I fix things the Weavers break."

"They break people often?"

"More often than they should." Kira picked up the clay cup and refilled it from a pitcher. "The Binding is dangerous. Ten percent mortality rate. Another twenty percent end up catatonic or mad. You're lucky."

Lucky. Right. Marcus tried moving his hand again. This time it responded, lifting a few inches before exhaustion dragged it back down.

On his palm, in the center, a mark glowed faint silver. A circle bisected by three wavy lines—the same symbol he'd seen on the chest, on the house, carved into the ceiling above him.

"What is this?"

"Your binding mark. Proof the ritual took. It'll fade to a scar in a few days, but the channels stay open permanently." Kira traced a finger across her own collarbone, where a similar mark showed through her shirt. "Everyone who crosses between worlds gets one. Mine was voluntary. Yours... less so."

"Can I go back?" The question burst out before he could stop it. "To my world. To Vermont. Can I undo this?"

Kira's silence was answer enough.

"The Binding is permanent," she said finally. "And even if it wasn't, the Weavers don't make mistakes. They chose you for a reason."

"What reason?"

"I don't know." She stood, gathering the empty cup and pitcher. "The Weavers don't share their plans with Menders. We're tools, same as you. We serve the pattern they're weaving."

Marcus's chest tightened—not from pain this time, but from something darker. Trapped. That's what he was. Pulled from his world, marked with magic he didn't want, bound to a purpose no one would explain.

"How long have I been here?" he asked.

"Three days."

His mother's face flashed in his mind. She'd be frantic by now. Police reports, search parties, blame. She'd blame herself, add Marcus to the list of things she'd lost.

"I have to get back," he said. "I have to at least tell her I'm alive."

"You can't." Kira's voice was gentle but firm. "The portal that brought you here sealed after you passed through. The house is dead now, cut off from this world. There's no way back."

The words hit like fists. No way back. Trapped in an alien world with magic burning through his veins and faceless things that called him a key.

Marcus closed his eyes. Breathed. The burning in his chest had faded to a dull warmth, almost comfortable now. His fingers responded when he flexed them. Strength was returning, bit by bit.

"When can I move?" he asked.

"Another hour. Maybe two." Kira moved toward the beaded curtain. "Rest. The Weavers will want to see you once you're on your feet."

"Wait." Marcus opened his eyes. "You said the Binding creates channels for magic. Does that mean I can use it? Cast spells or whatever?"

Kira paused in the doorway, silhouetted against brighter light beyond.

"Eventually. With training. But raw channels are dangerous—magic flowing through untrained paths burns the user from inside. Most keys don't survive long enough to learn control." She glanced back at him. "Don't try anything while I'm gone. I'd hate to come back and find you've immolated yourself."

She pushed through the curtain and disappeared.

Marcus lay in the silence, staring at the glowing symbols above. His palm still tingled where the mark had been burned into his skin. He lifted his hand, examining the silvery scar in the brazier's blue light.

Power. That's what she'd said the Binding gave him. Access to magic.

But at what cost?

The curtain rustled. Marcus's head snapped toward it, expecting Kira to return.

A Weaver glided through instead.

Taller than the ones in the forest, or maybe the low ceiling made it seem that way. Its cloak rippled like living shadow. Where its face should be, that same empty void, dark as the space between stars.

Marcus's Their breath came short and fast. His heart hammered against his ribs.

The Weaver approached the altar. Its movements were too smooth, too fluid—not walking so much as floating across the stone floor. It stopped next to Marcus's head and looked down at him.

Looked. Even though it had no eyes.

Cold radiated from it. Not temperature—something deeper, fundamental. Like standing too close to a black hole.

The Weaver raised one pale hand. Six fingers, Marcus counted. Maybe seven. They blurred when he tried to focus on them.

It pressed its palm against Marcus's forehead.

The world exploded into light.

Images flooded his mind: A city of silver towers reaching into violet sky. Gardens that grew without soil, flowers blooming in mid-air. Rivers of light instead of water, flowing upward into clouds. People—humans and others—walking streets paved with something that looked like solidified moonlight.

Then darkness. The towers crumbling. The gardens withering. The rivers running red.

War.

Not soldiers with guns, but beings made of light fighting creatures of shadow. Magic tearing reality apart at the seams. Thousands dying. Tens of thousands. The Veil being raised, cutting worlds apart to stop the bloodshed.

And in the center of it all, a figure Marcus almost recognized. A boy not much older than him, standing at a crossroads between worlds, holding something that burned brighter than the sun.

The key.

The vision shattered.

Marcus sucked in air, gasping like he'd been underwater. The Weaver's hand was gone. The creature stood motionless beside the altar, watching him with that terrible absence of a face.

"What—" Marcus's voice cracked. "What was that?"

The Weaver's voice spoke directly in his mind, layered and echoing:

*Memory. Warning. Choice.*

"I don't understand."

*You will.*

The Weaver turned and glided toward the exit.

"Wait!" Marcus struggled against the paralysis, managed to lift his torso a few inches. "What do you want from me? Why did you bind me?"

The Weaver paused at the curtain.

*The Veil is breaking. The war will return. You are the key to stopping it.*

"How? I don't know anything about magic or wars or—"

*You will learn. Or you will die. Either serves the pattern.*

The Weaver vanished through the curtain, leaving Marcus alone with those words echoing in his skull.

Either serves the pattern.

Marcus slumped back against the cold stone. His whole body shook. The mark on his palm burned hot enough that he had to clench his fist to bear it.

A war that ended by splitting worlds apart. A barrier that was breaking. And somehow, he was supposed to fix it.

A fifteen-year-old kid who'd never even finished his sophomore year.

Marcus laughed. The sound came out bitter and cracked. This was insane. All of it. He should be in algebra class right now, or walking home past the house on Willow Street, or eating microwaved leftovers while Mom got ready for her shift.

Instead he was trapped in a stone chamber beneath an alien forest with magic carved into his flesh and the weight of two worlds on his shoulders.

The burning in his chest pulsed once, sharp and fierce, then settled to a steady warmth.

Marcus looked at his marked palm. Three wavy lines bisecting a circle. The symbol of whatever he'd become.

He closed his fist.

If he was stuck here—if there was no way home, no escape from this Binding—then he needed answers. Real ones. Not cryptic warnings from faceless creatures or gentle lies from golden-eyed Menders.

He needed to know what the Weavers wanted. What this war was. Why the Veil was breaking.

And most importantly: how to survive long enough to find out.

The mark flared bright for a heartbeat, then dimmed.

Marcus lay back and closed his eyes, counting his breaths, waiting for strength to return.

One hour, Kira had said. Two at most.

Then the real test would begin.
