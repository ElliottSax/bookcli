# Chapter 8

The briefing chamber felt too small for what it contained.

Six keys. Five Weaver Elders. And six assignments that would determine whether the Veil held or shattered completely.

The center Elder's telepathic voice was cold and precise:

*Sarai. Northern corruption node. Reality bleed causing temporal distortions. Seal the breach. Eliminate any hosts beyond recovery.*

Sarai's jaw tightened, but she nodded.

*Paulo. Western node. Earth-binding gone wrong. A key attempted to fuse with bedrock and created a spreading petrification effect. Cut the thread before it reaches populated areas.*

Paulo's hands clenched into fists.

*Jin. Southern node. Phase-corruption. A Mender tried to help someone stuck between solid states and became trapped with them. Both are deteriorating. Mercy kill if separation impossible.*

Jin's face went pale, but he didn't protest.

*Dmitri. Eastern node. Lightning storm that won't disperse. Two keys fused during combat and created a permanent energy discharge. Find the fusion point. Separate or destroy.*

Dmitri met Marcus's eyes briefly. A look that said *this is what happened to my brother.*

*Lily. Central node, underwater location. Water-bond with deceased family member. Host is drowning herself repeatedly trying to resurrect her sister. Stop the cycle.*

Lily's Their breath came short and fast. "Will I have to—"

*You will do what is necessary. All of you will.* The Elder turned to Marcus. *And you. The primary corruption. The Veil itself.*

A holographic image appeared between them—two massive reality sections pressing against a failing barrier. The corruption visible as black tendrils eating through the Veil from the inside.

*The bridge exists in between-space. Neither your reality nor ours. You will enter through the weakest point in the Veil. Find the fusion that started the corruption. Remove it. By healing or by cutting—the method is irrelevant. Only results matter.*

"And if I fail?" Marcus asked.

*The Veil collapses within weeks. The war returns. Millions die in the first hour. Billions within days.* No emotion in the telepathic voice. facts. *Do not fail.*

The Elder gestured. Six items appeared, floating before each key. Small crystals, each one glowing with different colors.

*Return . When your mission is complete—or if you require emergency extraction—crush the crystal. A portal will open. You will have thirty seconds to reach it before it closes.*

Marcus took his crystal. It pulsed warm against his palm, in rhythm with his binding mark.

*You depart at sunset. Prepare yourselves.* The Elders glided from the chamber, leaving the six keys alone with their assignments.

For a long moment, no one spoke.

Then Paulo laughed. Harsh, bitter sound. "They're sending us to die, you know that, right? Six barely-trained keys against corruptions that stumped experienced Weavers."

"We're expendable," Sarai said quietly. "If we succeed, the Weavers get results without risking their own. If we fail, they call us failed experiments and move on to the next batch of keys."

"That's not—" Lily started.

"It is," Dmitri interrupted. "My brother was expendable too. So was the key before him. And the one before that." His voice was flat. "But we're going anyway. Because the alternative is worse."

Jin studied his crystal. "If we all fail, who stops the Veil from breaking?"

No one had an answer for that.

Marcus turned his crystal over in his hands. "We don't fail. We can't afford to."

"Easy for you to say," Paulo muttered. "You've been training with Evan. Learning advanced techniques. Rest of us got standard combat protocols."

"I can teach you," Marcus said immediately. "What I learned from Evan. Pattern reading. How to assess tangles before jumping straight to killing."

"In half a day?" Sarai shook her head. "Evan spent years developing those techniques. You've had weeks. We'd have hours."

"Better than nothing."

They looked at each other. Six people who'd trained together, bled together, survived when others hadn't. Now being sent to die separately in service of a war they didn't start.

"Teach us," Lily said. "Whatever you can. Please."

* * *

They spent the afternoon in the training chamber.

Marcus showed them what Evan had taught him. How to extend awareness into the Pattern. How to see the threads—not the full cosmic weave, but localized sections. The tangles they'd be facing.

"Look for the wound," Marcus explained, demonstrating on a pattern fragment Evan had lent him. "The original damage point. Everything else is spread. If you can find where the corruption started, you have a choice. Seal the wound and let the infection heal naturally. Or cut the source thread and cauterize the damage."

"What's the difference?" Paulo asked.

"Sealing takes longer but saves the host. Cutting is faster but kills them." Marcus met each of their eyes. "If you have time, if the situation allows it, try sealing first. But if the corruption is spreading too fast, or if the host is already gone—cut clean and get out."

They practiced on fragments. Learning to distinguish healthy threads from corrupted ones. Finding fusion points. Identifying which threads were saveable and which were too far gone.

Lily picked it up fastest. Her water affinity gave her natural sensitivity to flow and connection. She could trace corruption patterns almost as well as Marcus.

Jin struggled. His phase abilities made him uncomfortable with solid connections. But he learned to identify critical threads—the ones that, if cut, would collapse the entire tangle.

Dmitri approached it like combat. Quick assessment, decisive action, clean execution. His version was brutal but effective.

Paulo and Sarai focused on protection. Building barriers around healthy threads to stop corruption spread. Creating containment fields that bought time for healing or cutting.

By the time the sun started setting, they'd each successfully diagnosed and theoretically treated a dozen pattern fragments.

"It's not enough," Sarai said, collapsing onto the floor. "We need months of practice, not hours."

"It's more than we had this morning," Marcus countered. "And it might be enough to make the difference."

Lily set down her fragment, hands shaking. "I don't want to kill anyone."

"None of us do." Dmitri's voice was gentle. "But if it's them or millions of others, the choice is clear."

"Is it?" She looked at Marcus. "You're going to try to heal your tangle. Why can't we heal ours?"

"Mine is old. Stable. It's been spreading for a thousand years, but slowly. Yours..." Marcus hesitated. "Yours are active. Fresh. Still in the aggressive infection phase. Healing that while it's actively spreading might not be possible."

"Might not be. But could be."

"Could be," he admitted. "If you're fast enough. Skilled enough. Lucky enough."

Lily nodded slowly. "Then I'll try. For as long as I can. And if I can't heal it..." Her voice dropped. "I'll do what's necessary."

The sun touched the horizon. Orange light spilled through the Sanctum's crystal panels, painting everything in amber and gold.

Their time was up.

* * *

Kira found Marcus in his alcove, gathering the few possessions he'd accumulated. A spare tunic. His return crystal. Nothing else. Everything from Earth was gone. Lost when the portal sealed.

"You're going," she said.

"Don't have much choice."

"You always have a choice." She sat on his bed. "You could refuse. Let them find another key. Another solution."

"And condemn millions because I was too scared to try?"

Kira's gold eyes studied him. "You've grown up fast. Two weeks ago you were a terrified kid pulled through a portal. Now you're walking into a suicide mission with your eyes open."

"I learned from the best."

She smiled sadly. "Evan taught you well. But there's one thing he can't teach. Can't prepare you for."

"What's that?"

"The cost of success." Kira stood, moved to the doorway. "If you heal the tangle, if you save the Veil, the Weavers won't let you go. You'll be too valuable. They'll send you on mission after mission, tangle after tangle, until you burn out or break."

Marcus hadn't thought about that. About what came after success.

"And if you fail?"

"If you fail, you die. Or become corrupted. Either way, you're free." She touched the curtain. "I'm not saying you should fail. I'm saying... make sure you know what you're choosing."

She left before he could respond.

Marcus finished packing. Kira's words echoed in his mind.

Free through failure. Enslaved through success.

But millions of lives hung in the balance. His freedom didn't weigh against that.

Did it?

He shook off the thought. Didn't matter. He'd made his choice when he agreed to train. When he learned Evan's techniques. When he decided to try healing instead of killing.

Now he had to see it through.

Marcus left the alcove and headed for the departure chamber.

The other five keys were already there. Dmitri checked his lightning manifestation. Lily meditated beside a small fountain. Jin phased in and out of solid form, testing his abilities. Paulo and Sarai stood together, quiet conversation.

Six Weaver Elders waited at the chamber's center, surrounding six portal frames. Each frame glowed with different colored light—destinations set, pathways prepared.

Marcus's frame pulsed gold and black. Beautiful and terrible at once.

The center Elder raised a pale hand.

*The time has come. You know your missions. You carry the tools for return. Do not waste them.*

The Elders began the activation sequence. Reality warped around each frame, twisting inward, creating tunnels through space and dimension.

Marcus moved to his portal. The golden-black tunnel stretched beyond sight, disappearing into impossible distance.

Somewhere at the end, the Veil. The bridge. The corruption that threatened everything.

And maybe—if Evan's theory was right—two people who'd been trapped for a thousand years, suffering in a connection they couldn't escape.

Marcus took a breath. Looked back at his friends.

Lily gave him a shaky smile. Dmitri nodded once, grim but solid. Jin bowed . Paulo raised a fist in salute. Sarai simply said, "Don't die."

Good advice.

Marcus stepped into the portal.

The world twisted. Colors inverted. Up became down became sideways became something without direction. His stomach lurched. His vision blurred.

And then—

Solid ground.

Not stone. Not earth. Something else. Smooth and gray and humming with energy that made his teeth ache.

Marcus opened his eyes.

He stood on a bridge. Not metaphorical—an actual bridge, carved from something that looked like solidified starlight. It stretched in both directions, disappearing into mist.

Above him, the Veil. A massive barrier of translucent energy, stretching infinitely in all directions. And through it, Marcus could see both realities. His world on one side—cities, forests, oceans. The Weaver world on the other—violet skies, silver towers, impossible geometries.

The barrier was failing. Cracks spread through it like spiderwebs. Black corruption leaked from every crack, dripping down onto the bridge.

Marcus looked ahead.

There, in the distance. A figure.

No—two figures. Standing at the bridge's center, wrapped in black tendrils. Fused together at the hands, the chest, the head. Two people forced into one twisted form.

The source. The original corruption.

Marcus's marked palm burned.

He started walking toward them.

Toward the tangle that had been suffering for a thousand years.

Toward the wound that would save millions or doom them all.

His first real mission had begun.
